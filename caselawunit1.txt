🧠 CASE LAW – NAND KUMAR CASE 1775
Facts: Maharaja Nand Kumar a prominent Indian figure brought corruption charges against Warren Hastings the first Governor General of Bengal. Soon after this accusation Nand Kumar was arrested and tried in the Supreme Court at Calcutta for forgery. The trial was conducted under English law even though the alleged offense occurred in India and involved an Indian subject. This raised serious questions about which law should apply and who had the authority to judge such cases.
Verdict: The Supreme Court convicted Nand Kumar and sentenced him to death by hanging. The execution was carried out in 1775. Many contemporaries viewed the trial as politically motivated and questioned the fairness of applying English legal standards to an Indian citizen for an act that may not have been considered a capital offense under local customs or laws.
Significance: This case brought into sharp focus the jurisdictional conflict between the Supreme Court and the executive authorities in colonial India. It exposed the ambiguities in the Regulating Act 1773 regarding the powers of the court versus the Governor General and his council. The controversy contributed to later reforms including Pitts India Act 1784 which sought to clarify the separation of judicial and administrative powers. The case remains a landmark in understanding the early tensions between colonial law and Indian subjects.
