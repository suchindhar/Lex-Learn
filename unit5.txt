UNIT 5 – DIRECTIVE PRINCIPLES OF STATE POLICY
Directive Principles of State Policy (DPSP) are contained in Part IV (Articles 36–51) of the Constitution of India. These principles are guidelines given to the State for governance. They aim to establish social and economic democracy. DPSP instruct the government to create welfare state.
Directive Principles are not enforceable by courts. However, they are fundamental in governance. The State must apply these principles while making laws. They guide policies and legislation.
The framers included DPSP to achieve social justice. Fundamental Rights provide political democracy. DPSP aim to establish economic and social democracy. Together, they create balanced Constitution.

Directive Principles are instructions given to government. They direct the State to promote welfare of people. These principles aim to reduce inequality. They promote justice and social welfare.
DPSP include:
•	Equal justice

•	Education 
•	Health 
•	Village panchayats 
•	Environment protection 
These principles guide government action. Though not enforceable, they are important.

PART 2
UNIT 5 – DIRECTIVE PRINCIPLES OF STATE POLICY
Directive Principles of State Policy are contained in Part IV of the Constitution of India. Articles 36 and 37 explain the meaning and nature of Directive Principles. These Articles clarify who must follow DPSP and whether they are enforceable.
________________________________________
Article 36 – Definition of State
Article 36 defines the word “State” for Directive Principles. It states that the term “State” has the same meaning as in Part III (Fundamental Rights). Thus, the definition of State under Article 12 applies to DPSP.
Therefore, the term “State” includes:
•	Central government 
•	State governments 
•	Parliament 
•	State legislatures 
•	Local authorities 
•	Other authorities 
This means all these bodies must follow Directive Principles while making laws. DPSP apply to entire governmental structure.
________________________________________
Example
State government making welfare scheme follows DPSP.
________________________________________
Article 37 – Nature of Directive Principles
Article 37 explains nature of Directive Principles. It provides that DPSP are not enforceable by any court. This means individuals cannot approach court to enforce DPSP. Courts cannot compel government to implement them.
However, Article 37 also states that DPSP are fundamental in governance. It is duty of State to apply these principles in making laws. Thus, though not enforceable, they are important.
This creates balance. DPSP are guidelines but not legal rights. Government should follow them.
________________________________________
Illustration
Fundamental Rights → enforceable
DPSP → not enforceable
But DPSP → important for governance
________________________________________
Non-Justiciable Nature
Directive Principles are non-justiciable. This means courts cannot enforce them. The reason is that these principles require resources and policy decisions. Implementation depends on government capacity.
However, courts may interpret laws in light of DPSP. Courts encourage government to implement them. Thus, DPSP influence governance.
________________________________________
Example
Court cannot order government to provide employment under Article 41. But it may interpret law considering DPSP.
________________________________________
Importance of Articles 36 and 37
Article 36:
•	Defines State 
•	Applies DPSP to all authorities 
Article 37:
•	Non-justiciable 
•	Fundamental in governance 
•	Duty of State 
These Articles explain nature of Directive Principles.

Socialist principles under Part IV of the Constitution of India aim to establish social and economic justice. These principles seek to reduce inequality and promote welfare state. Articles 38 and 39 are the most important socialist Directive Principles.
These provisions require the State to create a just social order. They focus on distribution of resources, equal opportunity, and prevention of exploitation.
________________________________________
Article 38 – Social Order for Welfare of People
Article 38 directs the State to promote welfare of people. It requires the State to secure social, economic, and political justice. The State must minimize inequalities.
Article 38(1) states that the State shall promote welfare of people by securing social order. This ensures justice in society. The State must adopt policies to reduce inequality.
Article 38(2) provides that State shall minimize inequalities in income, status, and opportunities. This promotes equality. The State must reduce regional and social disparities.

Article 39 – Principles of Policy
Article 39 provides important economic principles. It directs the State to ensure fair distribution of resources. It contains several clauses.
Article 39(a) – Citizens should have adequate means of livelihood.
State must provide opportunities for earning livelihood.
Article 39(b) – Ownership and control of resources should serve common good.
Resources must benefit society.
Article 39(c) – Wealth should not concentrate in few hands.
This prevents economic inequality.
Article 39(d) – Equal pay for equal work for men and women.
This ensures gender equality.
Article 39(e) – Protection of workers and children.
Health and strength must not be abused.
Article 39(f) – Children should develop in healthy conditions.
Childhood must be protected.

Article 44 directs the State to secure a Uniform Civil Code for citizens. Civil laws relate to marriage, divorce, adoption, and inheritance. Currently, different religious communities follow different personal laws. Article 44 aims to create uniform law applicable to all.
Uniform Civil Code promotes national integration. It ensures equality. It removes discrimination based on religion. However, implementation is gradual because of diversity.
This provision reflects secularism and equality.

PART 10 
UNIT 5 – DIRECTIVE PRINCIPLES OF STATE POLICY
Directive Principles of State Policy in Part IV of the Constitution of India are different from Fundamental Rights in Part III. However, both aim to achieve welfare state. Fundamental Rights protect individual liberty, while Directive Principles promote social and economic justice. Together they form core philosophy of the Constitution.
________________________________________
Difference between Fundamental Rights and DPSP
Fundamental Rights are enforceable by courts. If these rights are violated, individuals can approach courts. Directive Principles are not enforceable. Courts cannot compel government to implement them.
Fundamental Rights impose limitations on State. Directive Principles impose duties on State. Fundamental Rights are negative obligations, while Directive Principles are positive obligations.
Fundamental Rights promote political democracy. Directive Principles promote social and economic democracy. Both are complementary.
________________________________________
Illustration
Fundamental Rights → Individual liberty
DPSP → Social welfare
Both together → Welfare state
________________________________________
Relationship between DPSP and Fundamental Rights
Initially, Fundamental Rights were considered superior. If there was conflict, Fundamental Rights prevailed. Later, courts adopted harmonious interpretation. Both must be balanced.
Courts held that Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles are complementary. The State must implement DPSP without violating Fundamental Rights. Laws should harmonize both.
Thus, Constitution aims to balance liberty and welfare.
________________________________________
Case Law – Champakam Dorairajan Case
Facts:
Reservation in educational institutions was challenged as violating equality. The government relied on Directive Principles.
Judgment:
The Supreme Court held that Fundamental Rights prevail over Directive Principles. The law was invalid.
Significance:
Fundamental Rights considered superior initially.
________________________________________
Case Law – Kesavananda Bharati Case
Facts:
Validity of constitutional amendments affecting rights was challenged.
Judgment:
The Supreme Court held that Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles are complementary. Both are part of basic structure.
Significance:
Balance between rights and DPSP established.
________________________________________
Present Position
Now both Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles are equally important. Courts try to harmonize them. Welfare laws based on DPSP are upheld if reasonable.
Thus, Constitution aims to achieve social justice while protecting liberty.

Overall Conclusion of Unit 5
Directive Principles guide State in governance. They promote welfare state and reduce inequality. Though not enforceable, they influence laws and policies. Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles together achieve social and economic democracy.
