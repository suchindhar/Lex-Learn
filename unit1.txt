UNIT 1 – HISTORICAL BACKGROUND OF THE INDIAN CONSTITUTION
Part 1 – Introduction, East India Company Rule, Regulating Act 1773
This unit explains how the modern constitutional system of India gradually developed through British rule and legislative reforms, ultimately leading to the adoption of the Constitution of India. The Indian Constitution did not emerge suddenly in 1950. It evolved through a long historical process shaped by colonial governance, administrative experiments, political demands, and constitutional developments. Each Act passed by the British Parliament introduced new administrative mechanisms, legislative institutions, and governance principles. These gradually transformed India from a trading colony into a constitutional democracy. Therefore, understanding the historical background is essential to understand why the Constitution contains features such as federalism, parliamentary government, judicial review, and fundamental rights.
________________________________________
Introduction to Constitutional Development in India
The constitutional development of India began with the establishment of the East India Company. Initially, the Company entered India only for trade. However, over time, it began acquiring political control over territories. As the Company’s power increased, the British Parliament became concerned about mismanagement, corruption, and lack of accountability. This led to the enactment of several laws to regulate administration in India. These laws gradually introduced centralized governance, legislative councils, representation, and responsible government.
The evolution of constitutional governance in India can be divided into three phases. The first phase involved Company rule from 1773 to 1858. The second phase involved Crown rule from 1858 to 1947. The third phase involved the framing of the Constitution by the Constituent Assembly. Each phase contributed important constitutional principles.
For example, centralized administration began with the Regulating Act. Legislative councils were introduced under Indian Councils Acts. Federal structure emerged under the Government of India Act 1935. Responsible government developed gradually through reforms. Thus, the present Constitution is the result of historical evolution rather than sudden creation.
________________________________________
East India Company Rule in India
The East India Company was established in 1600 by a royal charter granted by Queen Elizabeth I. Initially, the Company functioned as a trading body. It set up trading factories in Surat, Bombay, Madras, and Calcutta. Over time, the Company expanded its influence through wars and treaties. The Battle of Plassey in 1757 and the Battle of Buxar in 1764 strengthened the Company's political control in India. After these victories, the Company obtained Diwani rights in Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa. This meant the Company gained the power to collect revenue and administer civil justice.
However, the Company was not prepared to govern a large territory. Its administration was corrupt and inefficient. Officials exploited resources for personal gain. There was no clear separation between commercial and political functions. The Company’s servants accumulated wealth while the local population suffered. The Bengal famine of 1770 exposed administrative failures. These conditions forced the British Parliament to intervene.
The British government realized that a private trading company controlling large territories was dangerous. Therefore, Parliament enacted laws to regulate the Company’s administration. This marked the beginning of constitutional development in India.

Illustration
The Company originally came to India to trade spices and textiles. But after gaining revenue powers in Bengal, it started governing people. However, since it was still a trading body, governance became secondary. Officials focused on profit rather than welfare. This created confusion and exploitation. Therefore, Parliament stepped in to regulate administration.

Regulating Act, 1773
The Regulating Act of 1773 was the first step taken by the British Parliament to regulate the administration of India. It marked the beginning of parliamentary control over the East India Company. The Act aimed to control corruption, improve administration, and centralize authority.
Before this Act, the presidencies of Bengal, Bombay, and Madras functioned independently. This created administrative inconsistency. The Regulating Act centralized power by designating the Governor of Bengal as the Governor-General of Bengal. Warren Hastings became the first Governor-General. The Governors of Bombay and Madras were made subordinate to the Governor-General. This introduced centralized administration for the first time.
The Act also created an Executive Council of four members to assist the Governor-General. Decisions were taken by majority vote. This introduced a collective decision-making process. However, the Governor-General could be outvoted by council members, creating administrative conflicts.
Another important feature of the Act was the establishment of the Supreme Court at Calcutta in 1774. The court consisted of a Chief Justice and three judges appointed by the Crown. The Supreme Court had jurisdiction over British subjects and certain civil matters. This marked the beginning of a judicial system in India.
The Act also prohibited Company officials from engaging in private trade and accepting gifts. This aimed to reduce corruption. The Company was required to submit reports to the British government. Thus, parliamentary supervision increased.
Despite its importance, the Regulating Act had several defects. The powers of the Governor-General and Supreme Court were not clearly defined. This led to conflicts between executive and judiciary. The Supreme Court attempted to exercise jurisdiction over Indians, causing confusion. The Governor-General lacked effective control over subordinate presidencies.
However, the Regulating Act was significant because it introduced centralized administration, parliamentary control, and judicial institutions. It marked the beginning of constitutional governance in India.
________________________________________
Example
Before 1773, Bombay Presidency could take decisions independently. After the Regulating Act, Bombay had to follow directions from the Governor-General of Bengal. This shows the beginning of centralized governance.
________________________________________
Illustration
If three presidencies function independently, policies differ. But if one authority controls all, uniform administration develops. The Regulating Act created this central authority.
________________________________________
Case Law – Nand Kumar Case (1775)
The conflict between Supreme Court and executive became evident in the Nand Kumar case. Maharaja Nand Kumar accused Warren Hastings of corruption. Subsequently, Nand Kumar was tried by the Supreme Court for forgery and sentenced to death. This created controversy because the Supreme Court applied English law to an Indian. The case highlighted jurisdictional confusion between judiciary and executive.
Significance:
This case exposed defects in the Regulating Act and showed the need for clearer distribution of powers.


Part 2 – Pitt’s India Act 1784 and Charter Act 1813
________________________________________
Pitt’s India Act, 1784
The Regulating Act of 1773 introduced parliamentary control over the East India Company, but it failed to create an effective administrative structure. Conflicts arose between the Governor-General and his Council, and between the Supreme Court and executive authorities. The British Parliament realized that stronger control was required. As a result, Pitt’s India Act of 1784 was enacted to correct the defects of the Regulating Act. This Act introduced a system of dual control and strengthened British government supervision over Indian administration.
The most important feature of Pitt’s India Act was the establishment of the Board of Control. This Board consisted of British government officials, including the Secretary of State and Chancellor of the Exchequer. The Board was responsible for supervising political affairs of the East India Company. While the Court of Directors continued to manage commercial activities, the British government controlled political administration. This created a system of dual government — commercial authority remained with the Company, while political authority shifted to the British government.
The Act also strengthened the position of the Governor-General. The Governor-General was given authority over the presidencies of Bombay and Madras in matters of war, diplomacy, and revenue. This further centralized power. The number of council members was reduced to three, making decision-making easier. The Act also required the Company to submit all communications related to civil, military, and revenue matters to the British government. This ensured effective parliamentary control.
Another important aspect of the Act was that it distinguished between political and commercial functions. Earlier, the Company exercised both without clear separation. Pitt’s India Act separated governance from trade. This marked a shift from purely commercial rule to administrative governance.
The significance of Pitt’s India Act lies in the fact that it established British government control over Indian administration while retaining the Company as an administrative agent. It strengthened centralized authority, improved supervision, and laid the foundation for future constitutional reforms.
________________________________________
Example
Before Pitt’s India Act, the East India Company independently made political decisions such as war and treaties. After the Act, such decisions required approval of the Board of Control. This ensured that British government supervised Indian administration.
________________________________________
Illustration
The Company became like a manager, while the British government became the owner. The Company executed administration, but the British government controlled policy decisions. This is called dual control.
________________________________________
Charter Act, 1813
The Charter Act of 1813 marked another important stage in constitutional development. The East India Company’s charter was renewed every 20 years by Parliament. During renewal in 1813, Parliament introduced major changes. The most important change was the abolition of the Company’s monopoly over trade with India. Until this Act, the Company had exclusive trading rights. The Charter Act opened Indian trade to private British merchants. However, the Company retained monopoly over trade with China and tea.
This Act also marked the beginning of British responsibility toward education in India. It provided that the British government should spend one lakh rupees annually for promotion of education and knowledge. This was the first time education became a state responsibility. Though implementation was limited, it laid the foundation for modern education policy.
Another feature of the Act was permission to Christian missionaries to enter India. Missionaries were allowed to promote education and religious activities. This contributed to social reform and spread of western education. However, it also created cultural conflicts in some regions.
The Charter Act of 1813 also emphasized that sovereignty over Indian territories rested with the British Crown. Though the Company administered India, ultimate authority belonged to the Crown. This clarified the political status of India under British rule.
The Act did not introduce major administrative changes, but it marked a shift in policy. The Company’s commercial role declined and administrative role increased. The British government began to view India as a territory requiring governance rather than mere trade.
________________________________________
Example
Before 1813, only the East India Company could trade in Indian goods. After the Act, private British traders could also trade in India. This opened Indian markets to British merchants.
________________________________________
Illustration
Imagine one company controlling all trade. Prices remain fixed and competition is absent. When trade opens to others, competition increases. The Charter Act 1813 introduced this competition.
________________________________________
Conceptual Importance
The Charter Act 1813 is important because it:
•	Ended Company’s trade monopoly 
•	Introduced education responsibility 
•	Allowed missionary activities 
•	Recognized Crown sovereignty 
•	Reduced commercial character of Company rule 
This Act marked the gradual transition from commercial administration to governmental administration.
Part 3 – Charter Act 1833 and Charter Act 1853
________________________________________
Charter Act, 1833
The Charter Act of 1833 is considered one of the most important constitutional developments during the East India Company rule. This Act transformed the Company from a commercial organization into a purely administrative body. It centralized legislative power and introduced administrative reforms which later influenced the structure of the Indian Constitution.
One of the most significant changes introduced by the Charter Act of 1833 was the designation of the Governor-General of Bengal as the Governor-General of India. Lord William Bentinck became the first Governor-General of India. This change centralized authority in India and ended the administrative independence of Bombay and Madras presidencies. The Governor-General of India now had complete legislative authority over the entire country. This was the beginning of legislative centralization.
The Act also created a centralized legislative council. The Governor-General’s council was given power to legislate for all territories in India. The laws made by this council were called Acts instead of Regulations. This created a uniform legislative framework for India. This central legislative authority resembles the present Parliament’s power to legislate for the entire country.
Another important feature of this Act was the abolition of the East India Company’s commercial activities. The Company ceased to be a trading body and became purely administrative. This marked the complete transformation of the Company from a commercial enterprise into a political authority. The Company now governed India on behalf of the British Crown.
The Charter Act of 1833 also introduced the concept of open competition for civil services. It stated that Indians should not be disqualified from holding public offices on grounds of religion, race, or place of birth. This provision was significant because it recognized equality in public employment. However, in practice, competitive examinations were conducted in England, making it difficult for Indians to participate.
The Act also created the office of Law Member in the Governor-General’s Council. Thomas Babington Macaulay became the first Law Member. He played a major role in codification of Indian laws. The Indian Penal Code and other codifications emerged from this period. This contributed to uniform legal system in India.
________________________________________
Example
Before the Charter Act 1833, Bombay and Madras presidencies could make their own laws. After this Act, only the Governor-General of India had legislative power. This centralized law-making authority.
________________________________________
Illustration
If three different regions make separate laws, confusion arises. But if one authority makes laws for all, uniformity is maintained. The Charter Act 1833 introduced this uniform legislative system.
________________________________________
Conceptual Importance
The Charter Act 1833 is important because:
•	It centralized legislative power 
•	Created Governor-General of India 
•	Abolished Company’s trade functions 
•	Introduced open civil services concept 
•	Began codification of laws 
•	Created uniform legislative authority 
This Act laid foundation for centralized legislative governance in India.
________________________________________
Charter Act, 1853
The Charter Act of 1853 was the last Charter Act passed by the British Parliament. Unlike earlier Charter Acts, it did not renew the Company’s rule for a fixed period. This indicated that the British Parliament intended to reconsider governance in India. The Act introduced significant administrative and legislative reforms.
One of the most important features of the Charter Act 1853 was the separation of legislative and executive functions of the Governor-General’s Council. Previously, the council performed both executive and legislative functions. The Act created a separate legislative council by adding six new members. This marked the beginning of a legislative body similar to modern legislature.
The Act also introduced open competition for civil services. The recruitment to civil services was based on merit through competitive examinations. This allowed Indians to participate theoretically. This reform introduced merit-based bureaucracy. Later, this system became the basis of modern civil services.
Another important feature was expansion of legislative council. The Act added representatives from Bombay, Madras, Bengal, and North-Western provinces. This introduced regional representation. Though these members were not elected, their inclusion increased participation in law-making.
The Charter Act 1853 also allowed the legislative council to discuss laws in detail. This introduced deliberative legislative process. Members could debate and amend proposals. This resembled parliamentary legislative procedure.
The Act also indicated that the East India Company’s administrative role was temporary. Parliament retained power to decide future governance. This eventually led to abolition of Company rule in 1858.
________________________________________
Example
Before 1853, the same council handled administration and law-making. After 1853, separate legislative members were added. This created a body similar to legislature.
________________________________________
Illustration
When one body both executes and makes laws, power concentrates. But when legislative members are added, debate increases. The Charter Act 1853 introduced this separation.
________________________________________
Conceptual Importance
The Charter Act 1853 is important because:
•	It separated executive and legislative functions 
•	Introduced competitive civil services 
•	Expanded legislative council 
•	Introduced representation 
•	Created deliberative legislative procedure 
This Act marked the transition toward representative legislative institutions.
Part 4 – Government of India Act 1858 & Indian Councils Act 1861
________________________________________
Government of India Act, 1858
The Revolt of 1857 marked a turning point in the constitutional history of India. The revolt exposed the weaknesses of the East India Company’s administration. The British Parliament concluded that a commercial organization was not suitable to govern a vast territory like India. As a result, the Government of India Act, 1858 was enacted. This Act abolished the rule of the East India Company and transferred the administration of India directly to the British Crown. Thus, Company rule ended and Crown rule began.
Under this Act, the Governor-General of India became the Viceroy representing the British Crown. Lord Canning became the first Viceroy of India. The title “Viceroy” signified that the Governor-General acted as a representative of the Crown. This change symbolized the transfer of authority from a private company to the British monarchy.
The Act also created the office of the Secretary of State for India. This was a powerful position in the British government. The Secretary of State was assisted by a Council of India consisting of fifteen members. This council advised the Secretary of State in matters relating to Indian administration. The Secretary of State exercised complete control over Indian governance. He could send orders directly to India.
Another important feature of the Act was centralization of administration. The Viceroy and Secretary of State exercised strong control over provinces. The Act did not introduce representation or responsible government. It strengthened executive authority. Indian participation in governance remained minimal.
The Government of India Act 1858 also introduced the policy of non-interference in religious matters. The British government promised to respect Indian customs and traditions. It also assured equal treatment under law. These assurances were intended to prevent further revolts.
The significance of the Government of India Act 1858 lies in the transfer of power from Company to Crown. It introduced centralized executive authority, established Secretary of State, and marked the beginning of direct British rule.
________________________________________
Example
Before 1858, India was governed by the East India Company. After the Act, India was governed by the British Crown. This shows the shift from private rule to government rule.
________________________________________
Illustration
If a private company runs a country, profit becomes priority. But when a government takes control, administration becomes more structured. The Act of 1858 brought this change.
________________________________________
Conceptual Importance
The Government of India Act 1858:
•	Ended East India Company rule 
•	Introduced Crown rule 
•	Created Secretary of State for India 
•	Introduced Viceroy 
•	Centralized administration 
•	Began direct British governance 
This Act marked the beginning of modern administrative control in India.
________________________________________
Indian Councils Act, 1861
The Government of India Act 1858 centralized administration but did not involve Indians in governance. The British government realized that some participation was necessary. The Indian Councils Act 1861 introduced limited Indian participation and legislative decentralization.
One of the most important features of this Act was restoration of legislative powers to Bombay and Madras presidencies. Earlier, legislative authority was centralized. The Act allowed provinces to make laws for local matters. This marked the beginning of decentralization.
The Act also introduced legislative councils. The Governor-General’s council was expanded by adding non-official members. Indians were nominated to the council for the first time. Lord Canning nominated Raja of Benaras and Maharaja of Patiala. This marked the beginning of Indian participation in law-making.
Another important feature was introduction of portfolio system. Under this system, different departments were assigned to different council members. This improved administrative efficiency. This system resembles the modern cabinet system where ministers handle departments.
The Act also gave ordinance-making power to the Governor-General. In emergencies, the Governor-General could issue ordinances without legislative approval. This resembles ordinance power under Article 123 of the Constitution.
The Indian Councils Act 1861 also recognized legislative decentralization. Provinces were allowed to legislate on local matters. This was the beginning of federal tendencies in Indian administration.
However, Indian participation remained limited. Members were nominated and had no real power. They could not control executive authority. Despite limitations, the Act introduced representative institutions.
________________________________________
Example
Before 1861, no Indian participated in law-making. After this Act, Indians were nominated to legislative councils. This introduced limited participation.
________________________________________
Illustration
If only rulers make laws, people’s voice is absent. When representatives are included, governance becomes participatory. The Indian Councils Act 1861 introduced this participation.
________________________________________
Conceptual Importance
The Indian Councils Act 1861:
•	Introduced Indian participation 
•	Restored legislative powers to provinces 
•	Introduced legislative councils 
•	Created portfolio system 
•	Gave ordinance power 
•	Began decentralization 
This Act marked the beginning of representative governance in India.
Part 5 – Indian Councils Act 1892 & Morley–Minto Reforms 1909
________________________________________
Indian Councils Act, 1892
The Indian Councils Act 1861 introduced Indian participation, but it was very limited. Indians were only nominated and had no real legislative power. As political awareness increased and the Indian National Congress began demanding reforms, the British government introduced further changes through the Indian Councils Act 1892. This Act slightly expanded legislative councils and increased Indian participation, though still in a limited manner.
One of the major features of the Indian Councils Act 1892 was the expansion of legislative councils. The number of members in both central and provincial legislative councils was increased. This allowed greater representation of Indians. Though members were still nominated, the Act introduced indirect election. Local bodies such as municipalities, universities, and district boards recommended members. The government then nominated them. This marked the beginning of electoral representation.
Another important feature was the right of members to discuss the budget. Members could ask questions and discuss financial matters. However, they were not allowed to vote on the budget. They also could not introduce supplementary questions. Thus, the power given was limited but significant.
The Act also increased Indian participation in governance. More Indians were included in legislative councils. Though they had limited authority, they could express public opinion. This created political awareness and encouraged constitutional development.
However, the Indian Councils Act 1892 had several limitations. Members could not vote on laws. Executive authority remained dominant. The election system was indirect and controlled by the government. Responsible government was not introduced. Despite these limitations, the Act marked an important step toward representative governance.
________________________________________
Example
A university could recommend a representative to the legislative council. The government would nominate that person. This was indirect election.
________________________________________
Illustration
If people choose representatives directly, it is democracy. If organizations recommend representatives, it is indirect representation. The Indian Councils Act 1892 introduced this indirect system.

The Indian Councils Act 1892:
•	Expanded legislative councils 
•	Introduced indirect election 
•	Allowed budget discussion 
•	Increased Indian participation 
•	Began representative institutions 
This Act marked the gradual development toward legislative representation.
________________________________________
Morley–Minto Reforms, 1909 (Indian Councils Act 1909)
The Indian Councils Act 1909, also known as Morley–Minto Reforms, introduced significant constitutional changes. These reforms were named after Lord Morley, Secretary of State for India, and Lord Minto, Viceroy of India. The reforms aimed to increase Indian participation in governance and respond to political demands.
One of the most important features of the Morley–Minto Reforms was the expansion of legislative councils. The number of members in central and provincial councils was increased substantially. For the first time, some members were elected. This introduced the principle of election in Indian constitutional development.
Another important feature was the inclusion of Indians in executive councils. Satyendra Prasad Sinha became the first Indian member of the Viceroy’s Executive Council. This marked the beginning of Indian participation in executive governance.
The most controversial feature of the Morley–Minto Reforms was the introduction of separate electorates for Muslims. Under this system, Muslims elected their own representatives. This meant only Muslim voters could elect Muslim members. This introduced communal representation. The British government justified this as protection of minority interests.
Members of legislative councils were given the right to discuss budget and move resolutions. Though executive authority remained dominant, members could debate public issues. This increased legislative activity.
However, responsible government was still absent. The executive remained accountable to the British government, not the legislature. Separate electorates created communal divisions. Despite limitations, the reforms expanded representation.
________________________________________
Example
Under separate electorates, Muslim voters elected Muslim representatives. Non-Muslims could not vote for those seats. This divided the electorate.
________________________________________
Illustration
If voters are divided based on religion, political unity weakens. Separate electorates introduced such division.
________________________________________
Conceptual Importance
The Morley–Minto Reforms 1909:
•	Expanded legislative councils 
•	Introduced elections 
•	Included Indians in executive councils 
•	Introduced separate electorates 
•	Increased legislative participation 
These reforms marked an important step toward representative government but also introduced communal politics.
Part 6 – Montagu–Chelmsford Reforms 1919 (Government of India Act 1919) & Dyarchy
________________________________________
Background of Government of India Act, 1919
After the Morley–Minto Reforms of 1909, Indian political leaders demanded further constitutional reforms. The First World War also influenced British policy. The British government promised gradual development of self-governing institutions in India. In 1917, the Secretary of State for India, Edwin Montagu, announced that the objective of British policy was to introduce responsible government in India. Based on this declaration, the Montagu–Chelmsford Reforms were introduced, leading to the Government of India Act, 1919.
The Government of India Act 1919 marked a significant step toward responsible government. It introduced constitutional reforms at both central and provincial levels. The Act expanded legislative councils, introduced bicameralism at the centre, increased Indian participation, and introduced the system of dyarchy in provinces.
________________________________________
Bicameral Legislature at the Centre
One of the major features of the Government of India Act 1919 was the introduction of bicameral legislature at the centre. The central legislature consisted of two houses — Council of State and Legislative Assembly. The Council of State functioned as the upper house, while the Legislative Assembly functioned as the lower house. This was the beginning of bicameralism in India.
Members of both houses were partly elected and partly nominated. This introduced electoral representation. The legislature could discuss budget and pass laws. However, the Governor-General retained overriding powers. He could veto bills and issue ordinances. Therefore, the legislature did not have full authority.
________________________________________
Illustration
Two houses were created similar to modern Parliament:
Upper House → Council of State
Lower House → Legislative Assembly
This system later influenced:
Rajya Sabha → Upper House
Lok Sabha → Lower House
________________________________________
Dyarchy in Provinces
The most important feature of the Government of India Act 1919 was the introduction of dyarchy in provinces. Dyarchy means dual government. Under this system, provincial subjects were divided into two categories — reserved subjects and transferred subjects.
Reserved subjects were administered by the Governor and his executive council. These subjects included police, finance, revenue, and law and order. These were important areas of administration. The Governor was not responsible to the legislature in these matters.
Transferred subjects were administered by Indian ministers responsible to legislative councils. These subjects included education, public health, agriculture, and local government. Ministers were accountable to the legislature. This introduced partial responsible government.
Thus, administration in provinces was divided between British officials and Indian ministers. This system aimed to train Indians in governance.
________________________________________
Example
Education → transferred subject → Indian minister handled
Police → reserved subject → Governor handled
This shows division of power.
________________________________________
Illustration
Imagine a government where important departments are kept by one authority and less important departments given to another. This is dyarchy. Indians handled minor subjects while British retained major control.
________________________________________
Legislative Powers under 1919 Act
The Act expanded legislative powers. Members could ask questions, discuss budget, and introduce resolutions. Franchise was extended to more people. However, voting rights were limited. Property and educational qualifications were required to vote. Therefore, only a small portion of population could participate.
The Governor-General retained overriding authority. He could veto laws and certify bills. He also had ordinance power. Thus, executive authority remained dominant.
________________________________________
Provincial Autonomy (Limited Form)
Though full provincial autonomy was not introduced, the Act laid foundation for it. Provinces received some powers. Legislative councils were expanded. Indian ministers handled transferred subjects. This gradually prepared provinces for responsible government.
________________________________________
Defects of Dyarchy
Dyarchy created administrative confusion. Ministers handled transferred subjects but lacked financial control. Reserved subjects controlled finance and police. Therefore, ministers could not implement policies effectively. Governors retained overriding powers. This limited responsible government.
Another defect was division of authority. Coordination between reserved and transferred subjects was difficult. This led to inefficiency. As a result, dyarchy failed in practice.
________________________________________
Conceptual Importance
Government of India Act 1919 introduced:
•	Bicameral legislature at centre 
•	Dyarchy in provinces 
•	Limited responsible government 
•	Expanded legislative councils 
•	Increased Indian participation 
•	Extended franchise 
This Act marked transition toward responsible government.
________________________________________
Historical Significance
The Government of India Act 1919 is important because:
•	It introduced responsible government (partial) 
•	It introduced dyarchy 
•	It created bicameral legislature 
•	It increased political participation 
•	It prepared ground for 1935 Act 
This Act was an important step in constitutional evolution leading to federal system later.
Part 7 – Government of India Act 1935 (Federal Scheme, Provincial Autonomy, Dyarchy at Centre)
________________________________________
Background of Government of India Act, 1935
The Government of India Act 1919 introduced dyarchy in provinces, but the system failed due to administrative confusion and limited powers given to Indian ministers. Indian leaders demanded responsible government and greater autonomy. The Simon Commission (1927), Round Table Conferences, and constitutional negotiations led to the enactment of the Government of India Act 1935. This Act was the most comprehensive constitutional legislation before independence. Many provisions of the present Constitution are based on this Act.
The Government of India Act 1935 introduced federal structure, provincial autonomy, division of powers, bicameral legislature, federal court, and emergency provisions. It was the longest Act passed by British Parliament for India.
________________________________________
Federal Scheme under Government of India Act 1935
The Act proposed the establishment of an All-India Federation consisting of British Indian provinces and princely states. The federation was voluntary for princely states. Provinces were automatically included, but princely states had the option to join. Since princely states did not join, the federation never came into existence. However, the idea of federalism influenced the later Constitution.
The Act divided legislative powers into three lists:
•	Federal List 
•	Provincial List 
•	Concurrent List 
The Federal List included subjects such as defence, foreign affairs, and currency. The Provincial List included police, public order, and local government. The Concurrent List included subjects where both centre and provinces could legislate. This division later became the basis of the Seventh Schedule of the Constitution.
The Act also introduced residuary powers. These powers were given to the Governor-General. This showed strong central control.
________________________________________
Illustration
Federal List → Centre
Provincial List → Provinces
Concurrent List → Both
This system is similar to present Constitution.
________________________________________
Provincial Autonomy
The Government of India Act 1935 abolished dyarchy in provinces and introduced provincial autonomy. Provinces were given independent authority. Provincial governments were made responsible to legislative councils. Ministers were appointed from elected members. This introduced responsible government at provincial level.
Governors, however, retained special powers. They could override ministers in certain matters. They also controlled finance and law and order in emergencies. Thus, autonomy was limited but significant.
Provincial autonomy allowed Indian leaders to form governments. Elections were conducted in 1937. Congress formed ministries in several provinces. This was the first experience of responsible governance.
________________________________________
Example
After 1937 elections:
Congress formed government in provinces like Madras and Bombay.
Ministers handled administration.
This was beginning of responsible government.
________________________________________
Dyarchy at the Centre
While dyarchy was abolished in provinces, it was introduced at the centre. Federal subjects were divided into reserved and transferred subjects. Reserved subjects were controlled by Governor-General. Transferred subjects were handled by ministers responsible to legislature.
However, since federation never came into existence, dyarchy at centre was not implemented. Still, it showed gradual movement toward responsible government.
________________________________________
Bicameral Legislature
The Act introduced bicameral legislature in some provinces. At the centre also, two houses were proposed:
•	Federal Assembly 
•	Council of States 
This influenced present parliamentary structure.
________________________________________
Federal Court
The Government of India Act 1935 established the Federal Court in 1937. The court resolved disputes between centre and provinces. It also interpreted constitutional provisions. This was the beginning of constitutional adjudication in India.
The Federal Court later became foundation for the Supreme Court of India. It introduced judicial review and constitutional interpretation.
________________________________________

Centre vs Province dispute → Federal Court decides
Interpretation of law → Federal Court
This resembles present Supreme Court.

Other Important Features
The Government of India Act 1935 also introduced:
•	Public Service Commission 
•	Reserve Bank of India 
•	Federal Railway Authority 
•	Emergency provisions 
•	Separation of powers 
These features influenced the Constitution.

Conceptual Importance
Government of India Act 1935 introduced:
•	Federal structure 
•	Provincial autonomy 
•	Division of powers 
•	Bicameral legislature 
•	Federal court 
•	Responsible government in provinces 
•	Emergency provisions 
This Act became the backbone of the Constitution.
Part 8 – Federal Court (1937) & Indian Independence Act 1947
________________________________________
Federal Court of India (1937)
The Government of India Act 1935 provided for the establishment of a Federal Court. Accordingly, the Federal Court of India was established in 1937. This was the first constitutional court in India. Before this, judicial appeals from India went directly to the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council in England. The Federal Court created a domestic constitutional adjudication system.
The Federal Court consisted of a Chief Justice and other judges appointed by the British Crown. The court had original jurisdiction, appellate jurisdiction, and advisory jurisdiction. It could decide disputes between provinces and centre. It also interpreted constitutional provisions under the Government of India Act 1935.
The establishment of the Federal Court introduced the concept of constitutional interpretation. The court ensured that authorities acted within their powers. This marked the beginning of constitutional supremacy and judicial review in India. Though appeals could still be made to the Privy Council, the Federal Court played a crucial role in shaping constitutional jurisprudence.
The Federal Court also clarified distribution of legislative powers. It interpreted federal, provincial, and concurrent lists. This strengthened federal governance. Many principles developed by the Federal Court later influenced the Supreme Court.
________________________________________
Illustration
If centre passes law beyond its powers → Federal Court decides validity
If province disputes centre → Federal Court resolves dispute
This is similar to present Supreme Court function.
________________________________________
Historical Importance
Federal Court introduced:
•	Constitutional interpretation 
•	Federal dispute resolution 
•	Judicial review 
•	Constitutional supremacy 
•	Independent judiciary concept 
The Federal Court later became the foundation for the Supreme Court of India.

Indian Independence Act, 1947
The Indian Independence Act 1947 marked the end of British rule in India. This Act created two independent dominions — India and Pakistan. The Act came into force on August 15, 1947. It transferred power from British government to Indian leaders. This Act laid the foundation for framing the Constitution.
One of the most important features of the Act was partition of India. British India was divided into two dominions. Each dominion had power to frame its own constitution. The British Parliament no longer had authority over India. This marked complete legislative sovereignty.
The Act abolished the office of Secretary of State for India. The British government ended its administrative control. The Governor-General became constitutional head representing the Crown but acted on advice of Indian ministers. Thus, real power transferred to Indian leadership.
Another important feature was that princely states were given freedom to join India or Pakistan. British suzerainty over princely states ended. States could accede to either dominion. This led to integration of princely states into India.
The Indian Independence Act 1947 also gave power to the Constituent Assembly to frame the Constitution. The Assembly became sovereign legislative body. It could make laws and draft Constitution.
The Act also ended the applicability of Government of India Act 1935, except as interim Constitution. Until the new Constitution was adopted, India functioned under modified 1935 Act.

Part 9 – Constituent Assembly of India (Formation, Composition, Committees, Drafting Committee)
________________________________________
Formation of Constituent Assembly
After the Indian Independence Act 1947, the most important task before Indian leaders was to frame a Constitution for independent India. The idea of a Constituent Assembly was first proposed by M.N. Roy in 1934. Later, the Indian National Congress demanded a constitution framed by Indians themselves. The British government accepted this demand through the Cabinet Mission Plan of 1946. Accordingly, the Constituent Assembly was formed to draft the Constitution.
The Constituent Assembly was not directly elected by the people. Members were elected indirectly by provincial legislative assemblies. Seats were distributed among provinces based on population. Representatives of princely states were nominated by rulers. The Constituent Assembly first met on 9 December 1946. Dr. Sachchidananda Sinha served as temporary chairman. Later, Dr. Rajendra Prasad was elected as permanent President of the Assembly.
Initially, the Constituent Assembly had 389 members. After partition, membership reduced to 299. Out of these, 229 represented provinces and 70 represented princely states. The Assembly included lawyers, scholars, administrators, and political leaders. This ensured diverse representation.

Working of Constituent Assembly
The Constituent Assembly performed two major functions. First, it framed the Constitution. Second, it functioned as legislative body. After independence, the Assembly also acted as provisional parliament. It passed laws and discussed national issues.
The Assembly worked through debates and discussions. Members discussed each provision in detail. Draft Constitution was examined clause by clause. Amendments were proposed and debated. This democratic process ensured careful drafting.
The Assembly took 2 years, 11 months, and 18 days to complete the Constitution. It held 11 sessions and 165 meetings. The Constitution was adopted on 26 November 1949. It came into force on 26 January 1950.
________________________________________
Committees of Constituent Assembly
To simplify drafting work, the Constituent Assembly formed several committees. These committees examined different aspects of the Constitution. The committees submitted reports which were discussed by the Assembly.
Major committees included:
•	Union Powers Committee 
•	Union Constitution Committee 
•	Provincial Constitution Committee 
•	Advisory Committee on Fundamental Rights 
•	Drafting Committee 
•	Steering Committee 
•	Rules Committee 
Each committee handled specific constitutional matters. This helped in systematic drafting.
For example, the Advisory Committee examined fundamental rights and minority protections. The Union Powers Committee determined distribution of powers. The Drafting Committee prepared the final draft.

Drafting Committee
The Drafting Committee was the most important committee of the Constituent Assembly. It was appointed on 29 August 1947. Dr. B.R. Ambedkar was appointed Chairman of the Drafting Committee. Other members included K.M. Munshi, Alladi Krishnaswamy Ayyar, N. Gopalaswami Ayyangar, B.L. Mitter, Mohammad Saadulla, and D.P. Khaitan.
The Drafting Committee prepared the draft Constitution based on reports of other committees. It examined constitutional provisions from various countries. The committee refined language and structure. Dr. Ambedkar played a major role in drafting.
The Draft Constitution was published in 1948. Members of the Assembly discussed it clause by clause. Amendments were introduced and debated. The Drafting Committee revised the document accordingly.
The Drafting Committee ensured that the Constitution included federal structure, fundamental rights, independent judiciary, and parliamentary system. The committee balanced flexibility and rigidity. It also ensured protection of minorities and social justice.

Importance of Constituent Assembly
The Constituent Assembly played a historic role. It framed a Constitution suited to Indian conditions. It adopted democratic principles and federal structure. It ensured protection of fundamental rights. It established independent judiciary.
The Assembly also ensured representation of different communities. Members debated issues openly. The Constitution reflected collective wisdom.
The Constituent Assembly finally adopted the Constitution on 26 November 1949. It came into force on 26 January 1950, creating the Republic of India under the Constitution of India.
Part 9 – Objective Resolution, Making of Constitution, Adoption, Enforcement
________________________________________
Objective Resolution
The Objective Resolution played a crucial role in framing the Constitution. It was moved by Jawaharlal Nehru in the Constituent Assembly on 13 December 1946. This resolution laid down the philosophy and guiding principles of the Constitution. It declared that India would become an independent sovereign republic. It also emphasized justice, equality, liberty, and fundamental rights.
The Objective Resolution stated that power and authority of the government would be derived from the people. It also declared that India would be a federation of states. The resolution emphasized safeguarding minorities, backward classes, and tribal communities. It also aimed to promote international peace and cooperation.
This resolution later became the basis of the Preamble of the Constitution. The ideals of sovereignty, democracy, republic, justice, liberty, and equality were derived from this resolution. Thus, the Objective Resolution guided the drafting process.

Making of the Constitution
The making of the Constitution involved detailed debates and discussions. After the Drafting Committee prepared the draft, it was placed before the Constituent Assembly. Members discussed each article. Amendments were proposed and debated. The Assembly considered thousands of amendments.
The Constitution was prepared after examining various constitutional systems. The framers borrowed useful features from different countries. Parliamentary system was adopted from Britain. Fundamental rights were inspired by USA. Directive principles were borrowed from Ireland. Federal structure was influenced by Canada. This comparative approach ensured a balanced Constitution.
The Constituent Assembly carefully debated issues such as federalism, fundamental rights, judiciary, and executive powers. Members expressed different opinions. These debates helped refine the Constitution.
The drafting process took nearly three years. The Assembly met for 11 sessions. It held extensive discussions. The Constitution emerged as a comprehensive document.

Adoption of the Constitution
The Constitution was finally adopted by the Constituent Assembly on 26 November 1949. On this date, members signed the document. This marked completion of drafting process. However, not all provisions came into force immediately.
Some provisions relating to citizenship, elections, and provisional parliament came into force immediately. The remaining provisions came into force on 26 January 1950. This date was chosen to commemorate the declaration of Purna Swaraj in 1930.
The adoption of the Constitution marked the end of colonial constitutional framework. The Government of India Act 1935 ceased to operate. India became a constitutional democracy.

Enforcement of the Constitution
The Constitution came into force on 26 January 1950. On this day, India became a Republic. The office of Governor-General was replaced by President. The Constituent Assembly became provisional parliament. Democratic governance began.
The enforcement of the Constitution established parliamentary democracy. It introduced fundamental rights, directive principles, and federal structure. The Constitution became supreme law of the land.
The Constitution created three organs of government:
•	Legislature 
•	Executive 
•	Judiciary 
It also established rule of law and constitutional supremacy. All authorities became subject to the Constitution.

Part 10 – Sources of Indian Constitution, Borrowed Features, Nature of Constitution, Transition to Republic
________________________________________
Sources of the Indian Constitution
The framers of the Constitution of India did not create the Constitution in isolation. They examined constitutions of various countries and adopted features suitable for Indian conditions. The Indian Constitution is therefore a combination of many constitutional traditions. However, borrowed features were modified to suit Indian society and governance.
One of the most important sources of the Indian Constitution was the Government of India Act 1935. Many administrative provisions were taken from this Act. These included federal structure, office of Governor, emergency provisions, public service commission, and administrative framework. The division of powers between centre and states was also based on the 1935 Act.
The British Constitution influenced the parliamentary system in India. The concepts of cabinet government, collective responsibility, rule of law, and legislative procedure were adopted from Britain. The Indian Constitution also adopted the idea of nominal executive and real executive. The President acts as constitutional head similar to the British monarch.
The Constitution of the United States influenced fundamental rights, judicial review, and independent judiciary. The concept of written constitution and supremacy of constitution were also inspired by USA. The procedure for removal of judges was also borrowed.
The Constitution of Ireland influenced directive principles of state policy. These principles guide governance and promote social welfare. The idea of socio-economic justice came from Irish Constitution.
The Canadian Constitution influenced federal structure with strong centre. Residuary powers were given to centre. This ensured unity and strong national government.
The Australian Constitution influenced concurrent list and trade and commerce provisions. The idea of freedom of trade within the country was borrowed.
The Constitution of South Africa influenced amendment procedure. The method of electing members of Rajya Sabha was also borrowed.
The Constitution of Japan influenced the concept of procedure established by law under Article 21.
Thus, the Indian Constitution combined features from multiple sources. However, it remains unique due to its comprehensive nature.

Borrowed Features Explained
The parliamentary system adopted from Britain means executive is responsible to legislature. The Council of Ministers is collectively responsible to Lok Sabha. This ensures democratic accountability.
Fundamental rights borrowed from USA protect individual liberty. These rights are enforceable in courts. Judicial review ensures protection.
Directive principles borrowed from Ireland promote welfare state. These principles guide government policies.
Federal structure borrowed from Canada ensures division of powers. Centre and states have separate authority.
Thus, borrowed features were integrated into Indian framework.
________________________________________
Nature of the Indian Constitution (Evolutionary Perspective)
The Indian Constitution reflects historical evolution. It combines federal and unitary features. It provides parliamentary democracy. It ensures fundamental rights. It establishes independent judiciary.
The Constitution is partly rigid and partly flexible. Some provisions require special majority for amendment. Others can be amended by simple majority. This balance ensures stability and adaptability.
The Constitution also establishes rule of law. All authorities derive power from Constitution. No authority is above law. Judicial review ensures constitutional supremacy.
The Constitution also promotes social justice. Directive principles guide welfare policies. Fundamental rights protect individuals. This balance ensures democratic governance.
________________________________________
Transition from Colonial Rule to Republic
The adoption of the Constitution marked transition from colonial rule to republican governance. Before 1950, India was governed under British Crown. After enforcement, India became sovereign republic. The President replaced Governor-General. Sovereignty shifted to people.
The Constitution established democratic institutions. Elections were introduced. Legislature became representative body. Executive became responsible to legislature. Judiciary became independent.
The Constitution also unified India. Princely states were integrated. Federal structure created unity with diversity. Fundamental rights ensured equality.
Thus, the Constitution transformed India into democratic republic.

Overall Conclusion of Unit 1
The Indian Constitution is the result of gradual constitutional evolution. Beginning with East India Company rule, British Parliament introduced various reforms. Each Act contributed to constitutional development. The Government of India Act 1935 provided structural framework. The Indian Independence Act 1947 granted sovereignty. The Constituent Assembly drafted the Constitution. Borrowed features enriched the document.
Thus, the Constitution reflects historical experience, democratic ideals, and constitutional wisdom. Unit 1 explains this entire evolution from colonial governance to modern constitutional democracy.
