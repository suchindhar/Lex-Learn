UNIT 3 – UNION AND ITS TERRITORY & CITIZENSHIP
The Constitution of India begins its operative provisions with Union and its Territory. Articles 1 to 4 deal with the political and territorial organization of India. These provisions explain the nature of India as a Union, the composition of territory, and the power of Parliament to reorganize states. Understanding this unit is important because it explains the structure of India as a political entity.
India is described as a Union of States. This means India is composed of states and union territories. However, the Constitution deliberately uses the word “Union” instead of “Federation.” This reflects the intention of the framers that India is an indestructible union, though states may be reorganized. The unity of India is permanent, but boundaries of states may change.
The Union and its territory provisions ensure that Parliament has authority to reorganize states, create new states, or alter boundaries. This flexibility allows the Constitution to adapt to political and administrative needs. Over time, India has created several new states through this power.
________________________________________
Federal Structure of India
India follows a federal system of government. Federalism means division of powers between central government and state governments. In a federal system, both centre and states have separate authority. The Constitution divides powers through Union List, State List, and Concurrent List.
However, Indian federalism is different from classical federalism. India has a strong central government. The Constitution gives more powers to the Union. This is why India is often called quasi-federal. It combines federal and unitary features.
The Constitution provides dual polity:
•	Central Government 
•	State Governments 
Both function within their own spheres. States have authority over local matters. Centre handles national matters. This division ensures efficient governance.
________________________________________
Illustration
Federal structure:
Centre → national matters
States → regional matters
Both work together.
________________________________________
Union vs Federation
The Constitution uses the term “Union of States” instead of “Federation of States.” This distinction is important. A federation is formed by agreement between independent states. In such systems, states have right to secede. But India is not formed by agreement. States do not have right to secede.
The Union of India is indestructible. States can be reorganized by Parliament. This ensures unity. The framers used the word “Union” to emphasize that India cannot be divided.
Thus, India is federal in structure but unitary in spirit.
________________________________________
Example
Parliament created new states like Telangana. States were reorganized. But India remained one Union. This shows indestructible Union.

Importance of Union and Territory Provisions
These provisions:
•	Define India as Union 
•	Explain territorial structure 
•	Allow creation of states 
•	Maintain national unity 
•	Provide flexibility 
They ensure India remains united while allowing regional reorganization.

This forms the foundation of territorial organization of India.
Article 1 of the Constitution of India lays down the basic structure of India. It defines the name of the country and describes India as a Union of States. Article 1 is very important because it determines the political identity and territorial composition of India. It establishes that India is one political unit consisting of several states and territories.
Article 1(1) states: “India, that is Bharat, shall be a Union of States.” This clause has three important elements. First, it declares the name of the country. Second, it describes India as a Union. Third, it indicates that India consists of states. Thus, Article 1 forms the foundation of territorial organization.
________________________________________
“India, that is Bharat”
The Constitution uses two names for the country — India and Bharat. The phrase “India, that is Bharat” means both names refer to the same country. The name “India” is derived from the river Indus and has historical international recognition. The name “Bharat” has ancient cultural significance and is found in traditional texts.
The framers included both names to reflect historical continuity and modern identity. The use of both names also represents unity of tradition and modernity. The Constitution does not give preference to one name over the other. Both are official names.
The use of dual names also reflects linguistic diversity. Different languages use different names for the country. The Constitution accommodates this diversity.

Republic of India
or
Bharat Sarkar
Both refer to same country.
________________________________________
Illustration
India = International name

Both together represent national identity.

Union of States
Article 1 describes India as a Union of States. This means India is composed of states. However, the word “Union” has special significance. It indicates that the Union is permanent and states cannot secede. The Union is indestructible though states may be reorganized.
The Constitution deliberately avoids the word “Federation.” In a federation, states come together by agreement. They may have right to withdraw. But India was not formed by agreement among states. Therefore, states do not have right to secede.
Parliament has power to create new states, alter boundaries, or change names. This shows that states are not indestructible. The Union remains permanent. This reflects strong central authority.
________________________________________
Example
States like Andhra Pradesh were divided to form Telangana. State boundaries changed. But India remained one Union.

Article 1 also explains that the territory of India consists of:
•	States 
•	Union Territories 
•	Acquired territories 
States have elected governments. Union territories are administered by the central government. Acquired territories include any area that India may acquire.
This classification helps organize governance. States handle regional administration. Union territories are centrally administered. This flexible structure allows effective governance.

Article 1 of the Constitution of India not only defines India as a Union of States but also explains the territory of India. The territory of India includes all areas over which the Constitution applies. Understanding the territory of India is important because it determines the geographical and political limits of the country. The Constitution clearly classifies the territory of India into three categories: States, Union Territories, and acquired territories.
________________________________________
Components of Territory of India
Article 1(3) provides that the territory of India shall comprise:
•	The territories of the States 
•	The Union Territories 
•	Any territory that may be acquired 
This means India includes not only existing states and union territories but also any territory acquired in future. This provision gives flexibility to expand national territory.
________________________________________
States
States form the primary units of the Indian Union. Each state has its own elected government. States have legislative, executive, and administrative powers. The Constitution divides powers between centre and states. States govern local matters such as police, public order, agriculture, and local administration.
States are represented in Parliament. They have elected legislatures. Governors act as constitutional heads. State governments function under the Constitution.
The number of states has changed over time. Initially, states were classified into Part A, Part B, and Part C states. Later, states were reorganized on linguistic basis. New states were created through constitutional process.
States enjoy autonomy but remain part of the Union. Parliament has power to alter boundaries of states. Thus, states are not indestructible.
________________________________________
Example
Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, Maharashtra, and Kerala are states. Each has elected government and legislature.
________________________________________
Illustration

But all states → part of Union

Union Territories
Union territories are directly administered by the central government. They do not have the same level of autonomy as states. The President administers union territories through administrators or Lieutenant Governors. Some union territories have legislatures, while others do not.
Union territories are created for administrative convenience. They may be small areas, strategically important regions, or territories requiring central control. Parliament has authority to make laws for union territories.
Some union territories like Delhi and Puducherry have legislatures and limited autonomy. Others like Lakshadweep are directly administered by the centre.
Union territories ensure flexibility in governance. The central government can directly manage these areas.
________________________________________
Example
Delhi, Puducherry, Andaman and Nicobar Islands, and Lakshadweep are union territories.

Illustration
States → elected government
Union territories → central administration

Acquired Territories
The Constitution also includes any territory that India may acquire in future. Acquisition of territory may occur through agreement, treaty, purchase, or conquest. Once territory is acquired, it becomes part of India.
The Constitution allows expansion of territory. Parliament can decide how acquired territory will be administered. It may become state or union territory.
This provision ensures flexibility. It allows India to incorporate new areas.
________________________________________
Example
If India acquires territory through agreement, that territory becomes part of India.

Article 2 of the Constitution of India empowers Parliament to admit new states into the Union or establish new states. This provision gives flexibility to expand the Union. It allows India to include territories that were not originally part of the Constitution. Article 2 deals with external admission or creation of new states.
Article 2 states that Parliament may by law admit into the Union or establish new states on such terms and conditions as it thinks fit. This means Parliament has wide discretionary power. It can decide conditions of admission. This power is not restricted. It allows political flexibility.
The admission of new states usually refers to territories that were not originally part of India. These may include foreign territories joining India. Establishment of new states may involve creating a new political unit from acquired territory.
This provision ensures that India can expand its territorial limits. It also allows integration of territories into the Union.
________________________________________
Admission of New States
Admission refers to inclusion of an existing territory into the Union. This usually happens when an external territory becomes part of India. Parliament can admit such territory as a state or union territory. The conditions of admission are decided by Parliament.
This provision was used when territories like Sikkim became part of India. Parliament enacted law to admit Sikkim into the Union. Thus, Article 2 provides mechanism for expansion.
Admission may also involve merger of territories. Parliament decides representation and administration.
________________________________________
Example
Sikkim was admitted into India and became a state. This shows admission of new state.
________________________________________
Illustration

________________________________________
Establishment of New States
Article 2 also allows Parliament to establish new states. Establishment refers to creation of new state from acquired territory. This differs from Article 3, which deals with reorganization of existing states. Article 2 deals with new territory.
Parliament has discretion to determine structure. It may create state with legislature. It may create union territory. This flexibility ensures efficient governance.
The power under Article 2 is wide. Parliament can decide boundaries, governance, and representation.
________________________________________
Difference Between Article 2 and Article 3
Article 2 deals with admission or establishment of new states from outside territory. Article 3 deals with formation of new states by altering existing states. Thus, Article 2 concerns external changes, while Article 3 concerns internal reorganization.
This distinction is important. Article 2 applies when new territory joins India. Article 3 applies when existing states are reorganized.

Article 3 of the Constitution of India gives Parliament power to reorganize existing states. This Article is very important because it allows creation of new states, alteration of boundaries, and change of names of states. It provides flexibility to adapt territorial organization according to political and administrative needs.
India is described as an indestructible Union but destructible states. This means states can be reorganized, but the Union remains permanent. Article 3 reflects this principle. Parliament has authority to modify state boundaries without requiring consent of states.
Article 3 provides that Parliament may by law:
•	Form a new state 
•	Increase area of any state 
•	Decrease area of any state 
•	Alter boundaries of any state 
•	Change name of any state 
Thus, Article 3 gives wide power to Parliament.
________________________________________
Formation of New States
Article 3 allows Parliament to create new states. A new state may be formed by:
•	Separation from existing state 
•	Union of two or more states 
•	Union of parts of states 
•	Combination of territories 
This flexibility allows Parliament to reorganize states based on language, culture, administration, or political demands. Several states have been created using Article 3.
________________________________________
Example

Jharkhand was created from Bihar.

These were created under Article 3.

Increase or Decrease of Area
Article 3 also allows Parliament to increase or decrease the area of a state. This means territory of a state can be expanded or reduced. Parliament may transfer territory from one state to another.
This power helps resolve boundary disputes. It also helps administrative convenience. Parliament can modify boundaries to improve governance.
________________________________________
Example
If part of one state is transferred to another, area changes. This is allowed under Article 3.
________________________________________
Alteration of Boundaries
Parliament can alter boundaries of states. This means geographical limits of states can change. Boundary changes may occur due to administrative reasons or political demands. This power ensures flexibility.
Boundary alteration does not affect unity of India. States remain part of Union. Only boundaries change.
________________________________________
Change of Name of State
Article 3 also allows Parliament to change name of states. Names may be changed to reflect cultural identity or linguistic preference. Parliament can enact law for this purpose.

This provision provides flexibility in territorial organization.
Article 3 of the Constitution of India gives Parliament power to reorganize states, but this power must be exercised through a specific procedure. The Constitution lays down steps that must be followed before forming a new state or altering boundaries. This procedure ensures that states are consulted, though their consent is not mandatory.
The procedure begins with introduction of a Bill in Parliament. However, such a Bill cannot be introduced without prior recommendation of the President. This requirement ensures that the central executive examines the proposal before legislative action. The President plays an important role in initiating the process.
After receiving the Bill, the President refers it to the concerned State Legislature. The State Legislature is asked to express its views within a specified time. This step ensures consultation with states. However, the opinion of the State Legislature is not binding. Parliament may proceed even if the state disagrees.
After receiving views of the State Legislature, Parliament may pass the Bill. Parliament may accept or reject suggestions of the State Legislature. Once passed, the Bill becomes law and reorganization takes effect.
________________________________________
Steps in Procedure under Article 3
The procedure involves the following steps:
1.	Proposal for reorganization of state 
2.	President recommends introduction of Bill 
3.	Bill introduced in Parliament 
4.	President refers Bill to State Legislature 
5.	State Legislature expresses opinion 
6.	Parliament passes Bill 
7.	Reorganization takes effect 
This procedure balances flexibility with consultation.
________________________________________
Illustration
Proposal → President recommendation → State opinion → Parliament decision → New state
This is Article 3 procedure.
________________________________________
Role of President
The President plays a key role in the procedure. A Bill under Article 3 cannot be introduced without President’s recommendation. The President also refers the Bill to State Legislature. The President decides the time within which the state must express its views.
The President acts on advice of Council of Ministers. Thus, the central government controls the process. The President ensures procedural compliance.
________________________________________
Role of State Legislature
The State Legislature is consulted but its consent is not mandatory. This is important. Even if the state opposes reorganization, Parliament can proceed. This shows supremacy of Parliament.
The consultation requirement ensures democratic participation. States can express concerns. However, final decision rests with Parliament.
________________________________________
Example
When Telangana was created, Andhra Pradesh legislature opposed division. Despite opposition, Parliament created Telangana. This shows state opinion is not binding.
________________________________________
Supremacy of Parliament
Article 3 gives wide power to Parliament. Parliament can reorganize states without requiring consent. This ensures unity of India. States cannot veto reorganization.
This power reflects strong central structure. It ensures territorial flexibility. It also prevents states from blocking national decisions.

Article 4 of the Constitution of India deals with supplemental, incidental, and consequential provisions relating to laws made under Articles 2 and 3. When Parliament creates new states or alters boundaries, several administrative and legal adjustments become necessary. Article 4 allows Parliament to include such adjustments in the same law. This ensures smooth reorganization of states.
Article 4 provides that laws made under Articles 2 and 3 may contain provisions relating to representation in Parliament, allocation of seats, and other incidental matters. This means that when a new state is created, Parliament can also decide number of representatives, administrative arrangements, and other details. This avoids need for separate constitutional amendments.
Thus, Article 4 acts as supporting provision. It helps implement changes made under Articles 2 and 3.
________________________________________
Supplemental Provisions
Supplemental provisions refer to additional arrangements necessary after reorganization. When a new state is created, several changes must be made. These include creation of legislature, allocation of seats in Parliament, and administrative setup. Article 4 allows Parliament to include these provisions.
For example, when a new state is created, seats in Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha must be allocated. Administrative institutions must be organized. Article 4 allows Parliament to make such arrangements.
________________________________________
Example
When Telangana was created, representation in Parliament was adjusted. This was done using Article 4.
________________________________________
Incidental and Consequential Provisions
Incidental provisions refer to minor adjustments required due to reorganization. Consequential provisions refer to changes that follow from reorganization. These include changes in boundaries, courts, services, and administration.
Article 4 allows Parliament to include these provisions in the same law. This avoids multiple laws. It ensures smooth transition.
________________________________________
Illustration
New state created → representation changes → administration adjusted
Article 4 allows all these changes.
________________________________________
Not a Constitutional Amendment
Article 4 specifically provides that laws made under Articles 2 and 3 shall not be considered constitutional amendments under Article 368. This is very important. Normally, changing the Constitution requires special majority. But reorganization of states can be done by ordinary law.
Thus, Parliament can reorganize states without following amendment procedure. This provides flexibility. Territorial changes become easier.
________________________________________
Example
Creation of Telangana did not require constitutional amendment. Parliament passed ordinary law. This is due to Article 4.
________________________________________
Importance of Article 4
Article 4:
•	Supports Articles 2 and 3 
•	Allows supplemental provisions 
•	Allows incidental changes 
•	Avoids constitutional amendment 
•	Ensures smooth reorganization 
This Article ensures flexibility in territorial organization.
The Constitution of India under Article 1 includes not only existing states and union territories but also any territory that may be acquired. This means India can expand its territory in future. However, the Constitution does not expressly mention how territory is acquired or ceded. Therefore, constitutional interpretation and judicial decisions have clarified this position.
Acquisition of territory refers to addition of new territory to India, while cession refers to transfer of Indian territory to another country. Both processes involve international law and constitutional principles.
________________________________________
Acquisition of Territory
Acquisition of territory may occur through:
•	Agreement or treaty 
•	Purchase 
•	Conquest (historically) 
•	Accession 
When India acquires territory, it becomes part of India under Article 1. Parliament can decide how such territory will be administered. It may become a state or union territory.
Acquisition of territory is considered an executive function. The government can enter into treaties with other countries. Parliament may pass laws to give effect to such agreements.
________________________________________
Example
If India acquires territory through agreement, that territory becomes part of India. It may later be organized as a state or union territory.
________________________________________
Illustration
Foreign territory → agreement → becomes part of India
This is acquisition.
________________________________________
Cession of Territory
Cession means giving away part of Indian territory to another country. This is more sensitive because it affects sovereignty and territorial integrity. Unlike acquisition, cession cannot be done solely by executive action.
The question arose whether Parliament can cede territory without constitutional amendment. This issue was examined by the Supreme Court.
________________________________________
Case Law – Berubari Union Case (1960)
Facts:
The Government of India entered into an agreement with Pakistan to transfer part of Berubari territory. The question arose whether this transfer could be done by executive action or required constitutional amendment.
Judgment:
The Supreme Court held that cession of Indian territory requires constitutional amendment under Article 368. The Court stated that Parliament cannot transfer territory by ordinary law. Since territory is part of India under Article 1, any change requires amendment.
Significance:
This case clarified that:
•	Acquisition can be done by executive action 
•	Cession requires constitutional amendment 
________________________________________
Illustration
Acquisition → no amendment needed
Cession → amendment required
This is constitutional position.
________________________________________
Constitutional Position
The Constitution allows acquisition of territory but does not explicitly mention cession. However, judicial interpretation has established that cession requires amendment. This ensures protection of territorial integrity.
Parliament has power to reorganize states under Article 3. But it cannot transfer territory to another country without amendment. This distinction is important.
________________________________________
Conceptual Importance
Acquisition:
•	Addition of territory 
•	Executive function 
Cession:
•	Transfer of territory 
•	Requires constitutional amendment 
Case law:
•	Berubari case clarified position 
Thus, acquisition expands territory while cession reduces territory, and both have different legal requirements.
Part II of the Constitution of India deals with citizenship. Articles 5 to 11 explain who are citizens of India at the commencement of the Constitution and empower Parliament to regulate citizenship. Citizenship is an important concept because it determines membership in a political community. Only citizens enjoy certain rights such as voting, holding public office, and other constitutional privileges.
Citizenship establishes a legal relationship between an individual and the state. It confers rights and imposes duties. A citizen owes allegiance to the country, and the state provides protection. Thus, citizenship defines political identity.
________________________________________
Meaning of Citizenship
Citizenship means full membership of a state. A citizen enjoys civil and political rights. Non-citizens may reside in a country but do not enjoy all rights. Citizenship determines eligibility for certain privileges such as voting, contesting elections, and holding constitutional offices.
Citizenship also creates duties. Citizens must respect the Constitution and follow laws. They contribute to national development. Thus, citizenship involves both rights and responsibilities.
The Constitution initially dealt with citizenship at commencement. Later, Parliament enacted laws regulating citizenship. Citizenship can be acquired by birth, descent, registration, or naturalization. It can also be terminated.
________________________________________
Example
Only citizens can vote in elections. Foreigners living in India cannot vote. This shows importance of citizenship.
________________________________________
Illustration
Citizen → full rights
Non-citizen → limited rights
This explains citizenship.
________________________________________
Single Citizenship
India follows the concept of single citizenship. This means every person is a citizen of India and not of individual states. Unlike some countries with dual citizenship, India provides only one citizenship.
In countries like the United States, a person may be citizen of both country and state. But in India, there is only national citizenship. This promotes unity. Citizens can move freely across states. They have equal rights throughout India.
Single citizenship ensures equality. Citizens are not treated differently based on state. It strengthens national integration. It prevents regional divisions.
________________________________________
Example
A person born in Tamil Nadu is citizen of India, not only Tamil Nadu. He can work in any state. This reflects single citizenship.
________________________________________
Illustration
India → one citizenship
No state citizenship
This promotes unity.

Importance of Single Citizenship
Single citizenship:
•	Promotes national unity 
•	Prevents regional discrimination 
•	Ensures equal rights 
•	Allows free movement 
•	Strengthens integration 
Thus, citizenship provisions define membership in Indian political system.
Article 5 of the Constitution of India deals with citizenship at the commencement of the Constitution. It determines who became citizens of India on 26 January 1950. Since India became independent and adopted the Constitution, it was necessary to define who were citizens at that time. Article 5 provided the initial framework for citizenship.
Article 5 states that a person shall be a citizen of India at the commencement of the Constitution if he has domicile in India and satisfies any one of the following conditions:
•	Born in India 
•	Either parent born in India 
•	Resident in India for at least five years before commencement 
Thus, domicile is the primary requirement. In addition, any one of the three conditions must be satisfied.
________________________________________
Domicile Explained
Domicile means permanent home. It refers to the intention of a person to reside permanently in a country. A person may live temporarily in different places, but domicile refers to permanent residence. Article 5 requires domicile in India.
Domicile involves two elements:
•	Residence in India 
•	Intention to remain permanently 
Both must be present. Temporary stay does not create domicile. The person must intend to make India permanent home.
Domicile is important because citizenship depends on it. Only persons with Indian domicile became citizens under Article 5.
________________________________________
Example
A person living in India permanently with intention to stay is domiciled in India. He qualifies under Article 5.
________________________________________
Illustration
Residence + intention to stay = domicile
This determines citizenship.
________________________________________
Three Conditions under Article 5
After domicile, Article 5 provides three alternative conditions.
1. Birth in India
A person born in India becomes citizen if domiciled in India. Birth establishes connection with territory. This ensures persons born in India become citizens.
________________________________________
2. Parent Born in India
Even if person not born in India, he becomes citizen if either parent born in India. This recognizes lineage. It extends citizenship to persons connected by descent.
________________________________________
3. Residence for Five Years
A person residing in India for at least five years before commencement also becomes citizen. This ensures long-term residents become citizens.
________________________________________
Example
Person born outside India but living in India for five years before 1950 became citizen.
________________________________________
Importance of Article 5
Article 5:
•	Defined initial citizens 
•	Required domicile 
•	Provided three conditions 
•	Established citizenship at commencement 
This Article created initial citizenship base when Constitution came into force.
Articles 6 and 7 of the Constitution of India deal with citizenship relating to migration between India and Pakistan. At the time of independence in 1947, large-scale migration took place due to partition. People moved from Pakistan to India and from India to Pakistan. The Constitution had to determine citizenship of such persons. Articles 6 and 7 address this issue.
________________________________________
Article 6 – Citizenship of Persons Migrated from Pakistan to India
Article 6 deals with persons who migrated from Pakistan to India. It provides that such persons shall be citizens of India if certain conditions are satisfied. The Constitution distinguishes between two categories of migrants:
•	Persons who migrated before 19 July 1948 
•	Persons who migrated after 19 July 1948 
This distinction was made because migration rules changed after this date.
________________________________________
Migration before 19 July 1948
Persons who migrated from Pakistan to India before 19 July 1948 became citizens automatically if:
•	They or their parents or grandparents were born in India 
•	They were ordinarily resident in India after migration 
These persons were treated as citizens without formal registration. The Constitution recognized them as citizens.
________________________________________
Example
A person who migrated from Pakistan to India in 1947 and settled permanently became citizen automatically.
________________________________________
Migration after 19 July 1948
Persons who migrated after 19 July 1948 had to satisfy additional requirements. They needed:
•	Registration by government 
•	Residence in India for six months 
•	Application for citizenship 
This requirement ensured verification of migrants.
________________________________________
Illustration
Before 19 July 1948 → automatic citizenship
After 19 July 1948 → registration required
________________________________________
Article 7 – Citizenship of Persons Migrated to Pakistan
Article 7 deals with persons who migrated from India to Pakistan. It provides that persons who migrated to Pakistan after 1 March 1947 shall not be citizens of India. This means those who left India lost citizenship.
However, there is an exception. If such persons returned to India under permit for resettlement, they could become citizens. This allowed returning migrants to regain citizenship.
Thus, Article 7 distinguishes between migrants who settled in Pakistan and those who returned to India.
________________________________________
Example
Person migrated to Pakistan in 1947 → not citizen of India
Person returned with permit → may become citizen
________________________________________
Difference Between Article 6 and Article 7
Article 6:
•	Migration from Pakistan to India 
•	Grants citizenship 
Article 7:
•	Migration from India to Pakistan 
•	Removes citizenship (with exception) 
This distinction reflects partition conditions.
________________________________________
Conceptual Importance
Article 6:
•	Citizenship for migrants to India 
•	Before 19 July 1948 → automatic 
•	After 19 July 1948 → registration 
Article 7:
•	Migrants to Pakistan not citizens 
•	Exception for returning persons 
These Articles determined citizenship during partition.
Article 8 of the Constitution of India deals with citizenship of persons of Indian origin who were residing outside India at the commencement of the Constitution. Many Indians had migrated to other countries before independence for trade, employment, or settlement. The Constitution provided a mechanism for such persons to become citizens of India.
Article 8 states that a person who or whose parents or grandparents were born in India, but who is residing outside India, shall be deemed to be a citizen of India if he registers himself as a citizen at an Indian diplomatic or consular office. Thus, registration is necessary for acquiring citizenship under this provision.
This Article recognizes connection by origin. It allows persons of Indian origin living abroad to become citizens. However, unlike Article 5, citizenship under Article 8 is not automatic. It requires registration.
________________________________________
Persons Covered under Article 8
Article 8 applies to persons:
•	Residing outside India 
•	Of Indian origin 
•	Whose parents or grandparents born in India 
These persons must register themselves at Indian embassy or consulate. After registration, they become citizens of India.
This provision ensures that Indians living abroad can maintain connection with India.
________________________________________
Example
A person living in Sri Lanka whose grandfather was born in India may apply for registration. After registration, he becomes Indian citizen.
________________________________________
Illustration

This is Article 8.

Registration is mandatory under Article 8. The person must apply to Indian diplomatic representative. The government verifies eligibility. After approval, citizenship is granted.
This requirement ensures proper verification. It prevents misuse. It also confirms intention to become citizen.

Article 8:

Articles 9, 10 and 11 of the Constitution of India deal with continuation, termination and regulation of citizenship. After defining who were citizens at the commencement, the Constitution clarified what happens if a person voluntarily acquires foreign citizenship, and who has power to regulate citizenship. These provisions ensure that citizenship is properly controlled.
________________________________________
Article 9 – Voluntary Acquisition of Foreign Citizenship
Article 9 provides that a person shall not be a citizen of India if he voluntarily acquires citizenship of a foreign country. This means India does not allow dual citizenship. If a person becomes citizen of another country, he loses Indian citizenship.
The principle behind Article 9 is allegiance. Citizenship requires loyalty to one country. Dual citizenship may create conflict of loyalty. Therefore, Indian Constitution prohibits dual citizenship.
Voluntary acquisition is important. If citizenship is acquired voluntarily, Indian citizenship terminates. If it is automatic, different rules may apply under law.
________________________________________
Example
If an Indian citizen becomes citizen of USA voluntarily, he loses Indian citizenship. This reflects Article 9.
________________________________________
Illustration
Indian citizen → acquires foreign citizenship → Indian citizenship ends
This is Article 9.

Article 10 – Continuance of Citizenship
Article 10 provides that every person who is or deemed to be citizen of India under previous provisions shall continue to be citizen subject to law made by Parliament. This means citizenship continues unless Parliament provides otherwise.
Article 10 ensures continuity. It prevents uncertainty. Persons recognized as citizens under Articles 5 to 8 remain citizens unless law changes their status.
This Article also gives Parliament authority to regulate citizenship. Parliament may enact laws affecting citizenship.
________________________________________
Example
Person recognized citizen under Article 5 continues as citizen unless law terminates citizenship.

Article 11 – Power of Parliament to Regulate Citizenship
Article 11 gives Parliament power to make laws relating to citizenship. Parliament may regulate acquisition, termination, and other matters. This Article enables Parliament to enact comprehensive citizenship law.
Using this power, Parliament enacted Citizenship Act 1955. This Act governs citizenship in India. It provides rules for acquiring and losing citizenship.
Article 11 ensures flexibility. Citizenship rules may change according to circumstances. Parliament can amend law when necessary.

Article 11 of the Constitution of India empowers Parliament to regulate citizenship. Using this power, Parliament enacted the Citizenship Act, 1955. This Act provides detailed rules regarding acquisition and termination of citizenship. The Constitution only defined citizenship at commencement, but the Citizenship Act governs citizenship thereafter.
The Citizenship Act 1955 provides five methods of acquiring citizenship:
•	By birth 
•	By descent 
•	By registration 
•	By naturalization 
•	By incorporation of territory 
These methods ensure flexibility in granting citizenship.
________________________________________
Citizenship by Birth
A person born in India may acquire citizenship by birth. The rules have changed over time. Initially, every person born in India became citizen. Later amendments introduced conditions relating to parents' citizenship.
Citizenship by birth recognizes territorial connection. It ensures persons born in India become citizens subject to conditions.
________________________________________
Example
Person born in India to Indian parents becomes citizen by birth.
________________________________________
Citizenship by Descent
Citizenship by descent applies to persons born outside India. If parents are Indian citizens, the child may acquire citizenship. This recognizes lineage.
This method ensures children of Indian citizens living abroad can become citizens.
________________________________________
Example
Child born in foreign country to Indian parents becomes citizen by descent.
________________________________________
Citizenship by Registration
Citizenship by registration applies to certain categories:
•	Persons of Indian origin residing in India 
•	Persons married to Indian citizens 
•	Persons residing in India for specified period 
Such persons may apply for registration. Government verifies eligibility. After approval, citizenship is granted.
________________________________________
Example
Person of Indian origin residing in India may apply for citizenship by registration.
________________________________________
Citizenship by Naturalization
Citizenship by naturalization is granted to foreigners who satisfy conditions. They must reside in India for specified period. They must have good character and intention to reside permanently.
Naturalization allows foreigners to become Indian citizens. Government grants citizenship after verification.
________________________________________
Example
Foreigner living in India for long period may become citizen by naturalization.
________________________________________
Citizenship by Incorporation of Territory
When a new territory becomes part of India, persons living there become citizens. Parliament decides conditions. This method applies when India acquires territory.
________________________________________
Example
If new territory joins India, residents become citizens.

The Constitution of India through Article 11 empowers Parliament to regulate termination of citizenship. The Citizenship Act, 1955 provides rules for loss of citizenship. Citizenship once acquired may be terminated in certain situations. This ensures that citizenship is regulated and controlled.
The Citizenship Act provides three modes of losing citizenship:
•	Renunciation 
•	Termination 
•	Deprivation 
These modes ensure that citizenship can end voluntarily or by law.
________________________________________
Renunciation of Citizenship
Renunciation means voluntary surrender of citizenship. A person who is of full age and capacity may declare that he wishes to give up Indian citizenship. After declaration, he ceases to be citizen.
When a person renounces citizenship, minor children also lose citizenship. However, they may resume citizenship later. Renunciation is voluntary.
________________________________________
Example
Person settling permanently abroad may renounce Indian citizenship.
________________________________________
Termination of Citizenship
Termination occurs when a person voluntarily acquires citizenship of another country. India does not permit dual citizenship. Therefore, acquiring foreign citizenship automatically terminates Indian citizenship.
This reflects principle of single citizenship. Loyalty must be to one country.
________________________________________
Example
Indian citizen becomes citizen of Canada → Indian citizenship terminated.
________________________________________
Deprivation of Citizenship
Deprivation occurs when government cancels citizenship. This applies mainly to persons who acquired citizenship by registration or naturalization. Citizenship may be deprived if:
•	Obtained by fraud 
•	Disloyalty to Constitution 
•	Assisting enemy during war 
•	Residence outside India for long period 
This ensures misuse of citizenship is prevented.
________________________________________
Example
If citizenship obtained by fraud, government may cancel it.
________________________________________
Overseas Citizenship of India (OCI)
India does not allow dual citizenship. However, India introduced Overseas Citizenship of India (OCI). OCI provides certain benefits to persons of Indian origin living abroad. OCI is not full citizenship. It is a form of long-term visa and limited rights.
OCI holders:
•	Can travel to India without visa 
•	Can reside in India 
•	Cannot vote 
•	Cannot hold constitutional offices 
Thus, OCI provides limited privileges.
