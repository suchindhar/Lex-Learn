Article 1 | Declares India as a "Union of States" and describes the territory of India comprising states, union territories, and acquired territories.
Union of States | Term used in Article 1 to emphasize that India is an indestructible union and states have no right to secede.
Article 2 | Empowers Parliament to admit new states into the Union or establish new states on such terms as it thinks fit.
Article 3 | Gives Parliament power to form new states, increase/decrease area, alter boundaries, or change names of existing states.
Article 4 | Provides that laws under Articles 2 and 3 may contain supplemental, incidental, and consequential provisions and are not constitutional amendments.
Berubari Union Case (1960) | Supreme Court held that cession of Indian territory requires constitutional amendment under Article 368; acquisition can be by executive action.
Domicile | Permanent home; for citizenship under Article 5, it requires residence in India and intention to remain permanently.
Single Citizenship | India follows one citizenship – every person is citizen of India only, not of individual states; promotes national unity.
Article 5 | Determines citizenship at commencement of Constitution (26 Jan 1950) – requires domicile in India and one of three conditions (birth, parent's birth, or 5 years' residence).
Article 6 | Citizenship of persons who migrated from Pakistan to India; automatic for migration before 19 July 1948; registration required after that date.
Article 7 | Citizenship of persons who migrated from India to Pakistan after 1 March 1947 – they lose citizenship, but can regain if return under permit.
Article 8 | Citizenship of persons of Indian origin residing outside India – can become citizens by registration at an Indian diplomatic or consular office.
Article 9 | Prohibits dual citizenship – voluntary acquisition of foreign citizenship ends Indian citizenship.
Article 10 | Continuance of citizenship – every citizen continues to be citizen subject to law made by Parliament.
Article 11 | Empowers Parliament to regulate citizenship – led to enactment of Citizenship Act, 1955.
Citizenship Act, 1955 | Provides five methods of acquiring citizenship: birth, descent, registration, naturalization, and incorporation of territory.
Renunciation of citizenship | Voluntary surrender of citizenship by a person of full age and capacity; minor children also lose citizenship.
Termination of citizenship | Automatic loss when a person voluntarily acquires citizenship of another country (no dual citizenship).
Deprivation of citizenship | Government cancels citizenship obtained by fraud, disloyalty, assisting enemy, or long residence outside India.
Overseas Citizenship of India (OCI) | Not full citizenship; provides long-term visa, travel without visa, residence, but no voting or constitutional office rights.
