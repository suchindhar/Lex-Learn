CASE LAW – M.C. Mehta v. Union of India (1986) (Oleum Gas Leak case)
Facts: A major oleum gas leak occurred from a factory in Delhi, causing environmental damage and health hazards. The Supreme Court was seized of the matter regarding liability for environmental harm.
Judgment: The Supreme Court held that the Fundamental Duty under Article 51A(g) to protect the environment imposes a constitutional obligation on citizens and the State. The Court expanded the concept of absolute liability for industries engaged in hazardous activities. It also directed that environmental protection must be treated as a priority.
Significance: This case established that Fundamental Duties, though non-justiciable, can be used by courts to interpret laws and impose obligations. Article 51A(g) became a powerful tool for environmental jurisprudence in India, leading to stricter pollution control and the polluter pays principle.

CASE LAW – AIIMS Students' Union v. AIIMS (2001)
Facts: The AIIMS Students' Union challenged certain service conditions and reservation policies. The question arose whether Fundamental Duties could be considered while interpreting constitutional provisions.
Judgment: The Supreme Court observed that Fundamental Duties are equally important as Fundamental Rights. While rights are enforceable, duties guide citizens and the State. Courts must read Fundamental Duties into laws and policies to promote national integration, excellence, and harmony.
Significance: This case clarified that although Fundamental Duties are not directly enforceable, they serve as a constant reminder to citizens of their responsibilities. Courts can refer to them to uphold reasonable restrictions and to balance individual rights with social interests.
