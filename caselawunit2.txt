Berubari Union Case (1960)
Facts: The Government of India entered into an agreement with Pakistan to transfer the Berubari territory. The question arose whether such transfer could be made under executive power, and the issue was referred to the Supreme Court for opinion.
Judgment: The Supreme Court held that the Preamble is not a part of the Constitution. It stated that the Preamble is only a key to open the minds of the framers and cannot be used as a source of power. Therefore, a constitutional amendment was required to transfer territory.
Significance: This case initially held that the Preamble is not part of the Constitution. However, this view was later reconsidered and overruled in the Kesavananda Bharati case.

Kesavananda Bharati Case (1973)
Facts: The validity of constitutional amendments affecting fundamental rights was challenged. The issue before the Supreme Court was whether Parliament has unlimited power to amend the Constitution.
Judgment: The Supreme Court held that the Preamble is part of the Constitution. The Court also held that Parliament can amend the Constitution but cannot destroy its basic structure. The Preamble reflects basic structure such as democracy, secularism, and sovereignty.
Significance: This case overruled the earlier view in Berubari Union Case. It established that the Preamble is part of the Constitution and that amendments cannot destroy its basic features, laying the foundation of the Basic Structure Doctrine.

LIC of India Case (1995)
Facts: In this case, the interpretation of constitutional provisions relating to state policy was questioned. The court examined the role of the Preamble in interpreting constitutional provisions.
Judgment: The Supreme Court held that the Preamble is an integral part of the Constitution. It emphasized that the Preamble reflects the objectives of the Constitution and should guide the interpretation of constitutional provisions.
Significance: This case reaffirmed the importance of the Preamble and its interpretative value, confirming that courts should rely on the Preamble to understand the spirit and objectives of the Constitution.
