UNIT 4 – FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS
Fundamental Rights are one of the most important parts of the Constitution of India. They are contained in Part III (Articles 12–35). These rights are essential for development of individual personality and for maintaining democratic system. Fundamental Rights protect citizens against arbitrary actions of the state. They ensure liberty, equality, and dignity.
Fundamental Rights are called “fundamental” because they are basic rights necessary for human development. They are guaranteed by the Constitution. The government cannot violate these rights. If rights are violated, individuals can approach courts for protection. Thus, Fundamental Rights are enforceable.
The framers of the Constitution included Fundamental Rights to protect individual freedom. During British rule, many freedoms were restricted. Therefore, the Constitution guaranteed these rights to prevent misuse of power. These rights ensure limited government and rule of law.
________________________________________
Meaning of Fundamental Rights
Fundamental Rights are basic human freedoms guaranteed by the Constitution. They protect individuals from state interference. These rights include equality, freedom, protection against exploitation, religious freedom, cultural rights, and constitutional remedies.
Fundamental Rights are not absolute. The Constitution allows reasonable restrictions. This ensures balance between individual liberty and public interest. Thus, Fundamental Rights promote democratic governance.
These rights are enforceable in courts. If government violates these rights, individuals can approach High Court or Supreme Court. Courts can strike down laws violating Fundamental Rights.
________________________________________
Example
If government restricts freedom of speech without valid reason, a person can challenge the law in court. Court may declare law invalid.
________________________________________
Illustration
State violates right → person approaches court → court protects right
This shows enforceability.
________________________________________
Articles Covered under Fundamental Rights
Fundamental Rights are contained in Articles 12 to 35. These Articles are grouped into six categories:
•	Right to Equality (Articles 14–18) 
•	Right to Freedom (Articles 19–22) 
•	Right against Exploitation (Articles 23–24) 
•	Right to Freedom of Religion (Articles 25–28) 
•	Cultural and Educational Rights (Articles 29–30) 
•	Right to Constitutional Remedies (Article 32) 
These categories cover civil, political, and individual liberties.
________________________________________
Nature of Fundamental Rights
Fundamental Rights have the following features:
•	Guaranteed by Constitution 
•	Enforceable in courts 
•	Binding on state 
•	Not absolute 
•	Subject to reasonable restrictions 
These features distinguish Fundamental Rights from ordinary rights. Ordinary rights are created by laws. Fundamental Rights are protected by Constitution.
Fundamental Rights also act as limitation on state power. Government cannot pass laws violating these rights. If such laws are passed, courts can strike them down.
________________________________________
Importance of Fundamental Rights
Fundamental Rights are important because they:
•	Protect individual liberty 
•	Promote equality 
•	Prevent misuse of power 
•	Maintain rule of law 
•	Strengthen democracy 
•	Protect minorities 
Fundamental Rights also promote human dignity. They ensure freedom of expression and belief. They allow individuals to develop personality.
These rights also act as safeguard against authoritarian government. They limit state authority. They ensure accountability.
________________________________________
Example
Freedom of speech allows citizens to criticize government. This strengthens democracy.
________________________________________
Conceptual Understanding
Fundamental Rights:
•	Basic rights 
•	Enforceable 
•	Protect individuals 
•	Limit government 
•	Promote democracy 
Thus, Fundamental Rights form backbone of democratic Constitution.

Fundamental Rights in Part III of the Constitution of India are enforceable mainly against the State. Therefore, it is important to understand what the term “State” means. Article 12 defines the word “State” for the purpose of Fundamental Rights. This definition is wider than ordinary meaning. It includes not only government but also authorities performing public functions.
Article 12 states that unless the context otherwise requires, the term “State” includes:
•	Government and Parliament of India 
•	Government and Legislature of States 
•	Local authorities 
•	Other authorities within territory of India or under control of Government of India 
Thus, Article 12 gives an inclusive definition. It expands meaning of State.
________________________________________
Government and Parliament of India
The term “State” includes central government and Parliament. This means actions of central government must comply with Fundamental Rights. Any law passed by Parliament violating Fundamental Rights can be struck down.
This ensures that central government does not misuse power. All executive actions of Union government are subject to Fundamental Rights.
________________________________________
Example
If Parliament passes a law violating freedom of speech, courts can declare it unconstitutional.
________________________________________
Government and Legislature of States
Article 12 also includes state governments and state legislatures. This means state governments must respect Fundamental Rights. Laws passed by state legislatures must comply with Constitution.
State authorities cannot violate Fundamental Rights. Courts can review state laws.
________________________________________
Example
If state government restricts equality unlawfully, the law may be struck down.
________________________________________
Local Authorities
Article 12 includes local authorities. Local authorities are bodies created for local administration. These include:
•	Municipalities 
•	Panchayats 
•	District boards 
•	Municipal corporations 
These authorities perform public functions. Therefore, they must follow Fundamental Rights. Actions of local authorities can be challenged.
________________________________________
Example
If municipality discriminates against citizens, affected persons can approach court.
________________________________________
Other Authorities
The term “other authorities” makes Article 12 very wide. It includes bodies created by statute or performing public functions. These authorities may include corporations, boards, or agencies controlled by government.
Courts have interpreted “other authorities” broadly. If a body is controlled by government, funded by government, or performs public duty, it may be treated as State.
This ensures that government cannot escape liability by creating separate bodies.
________________________________________
Illustration
Government → creates corporation → corporation performs public function
Corporation treated as State

Importance of Article 12
Article 12:
•	Defines State 
•	Expands scope of Fundamental Rights 
•	Includes government bodies 
•	Includes public authorities 
•	Ensures accountability 
Fundamental Rights are enforceable against State. Therefore, defining State is essential.

Article 13 of the Constitution of India is very important in the chapter of Fundamental Rights. It provides that any law inconsistent with Fundamental Rights shall be void. This Article establishes the principle of judicial review. It ensures that laws violating Fundamental Rights can be struck down by courts.
Article 13 applies to both pre-constitutional laws and post-constitutional laws. It ensures supremacy of the Constitution. Parliament and state legislatures cannot make laws violating Fundamental Rights.
Article 13 contains four clauses:
•	Article 13(1) – Pre-constitutional laws 
•	Article 13(2) – Post-constitutional laws 
•	Article 13(3) – Meaning of law 
•	Article 13(4) – Constitutional amendments 
________________________________________
Article 13(1) – Pre-Constitutional Laws
Article 13(1) provides that laws in force before commencement of the Constitution shall be void to the extent of inconsistency with Fundamental Rights. This means old laws inconsistent with Fundamental Rights become invalid.
However, such laws are not completely void. They become inoperative only to extent of inconsistency. This leads to doctrine of eclipse.
________________________________________
Example
A law passed before 1950 restricting freedom becomes invalid after Constitution.
________________________________________
Article 13(2) – Post-Constitutional Laws
Article 13(2) states that the State shall not make any law violating Fundamental Rights. If such law is made, it shall be void. This ensures that legislature cannot pass unconstitutional laws.
This clause strengthens judicial review. Courts can strike down laws violating rights.
________________________________________
Illustration
Law violates Fundamental Right → Court strikes down law
This is Article 13(2).
________________________________________
Article 13(3) – Meaning of Law
Article 13(3) defines “law” broadly. It includes:
•	Ordinances 
•	Orders 
•	Rules 
•	Regulations 
•	Notifications 
•	Customs 
This wide definition ensures all forms of law are subject to Fundamental Rights.
________________________________________
Judicial Review
Judicial review means power of courts to examine validity of laws. If a law violates Fundamental Rights, courts declare it void. Article 13 is basis of judicial review.
Judicial review ensures constitutional supremacy. Legislature cannot override Fundamental Rights. Courts act as guardian of Constitution.
________________________________________
Example
If law restricts liberty arbitrarily, court may strike it down.
________________________________________
Doctrine of Severability
Doctrine of severability means that only the unconstitutional part of law becomes void, not the entire law. If valid and invalid parts can be separated, valid part remains.
This doctrine ensures that entire law is not struck down unnecessarily. Only offending portion is removed.
________________________________________
Example
If one section of law violates rights, only that section is invalid. Rest of law continues.
________________________________________
Illustration
Law = valid part + invalid part
Remove invalid part → valid part remains
This is severability.
________________________________________
Doctrine of Eclipse
Doctrine of eclipse applies to pre-constitutional laws. Such laws inconsistent with Fundamental Rights are not completely void. They become dormant. If inconsistency is removed, law becomes valid again.
The law is overshadowed by Fundamental Rights. It is not dead. It may revive later.
________________________________________
Example
Old law inconsistent with rights becomes inactive. If rights amended, law may revive.
________________________________________
Conceptual Importance
Article 13:
•	Judicial review 
•	Invalidates unconstitutional laws 
Doctrine of Severability:
•	Only invalid part void 
Doctrine of Eclipse:
•	Pre-constitutional law dormant 
These principles protect Fundamental Rights.

Article 14 of the Constitution of India guarantees the Right to Equality. It provides that the State shall not deny to any person equality before the law or equal protection of the laws within the territory of India. This Article ensures fairness and non-arbitrary governance. It is one of the most important provisions in Fundamental Rights.
Article 14 applies to all persons, not only citizens. This means both citizens and non-citizens are entitled to equality before law. The Article ensures that the State treats individuals equally and does not discriminate arbitrarily.
Article 14 contains two important expressions:
•	Equality before law 
•	Equal protection of laws 
Though similar, they have different meanings.
________________________________________
Equality Before Law
Equality before law is a negative concept. It means absence of special privileges. All persons are equal before law. No person is above law. Everyone is subject to ordinary law of the land.
This concept is derived from British rule of law. It ensures that government officials and citizens are treated equally. No special courts or privileges exist.
Equality before law ensures legal equality. It prevents arbitrary action by government. It promotes rule of law.
________________________________________
Example
A government officer committing crime is punished like any other person. This reflects equality before law.
________________________________________
Illustration
No special privilege → all equal → same law applies
This is equality before law.
________________________________________
Equal Protection of Laws
Equal protection of laws is a positive concept. It means equal treatment in similar circumstances. Persons similarly situated should be treated alike. This concept is derived from American Constitution.
Equal protection does not mean identical treatment. It allows reasonable classification. Different groups may be treated differently if classification is reasonable.
This ensures fairness. Law may treat different classes differently if justified.
________________________________________
Example
Different tax rates for different income groups are valid. This is reasonable classification.
________________________________________
Illustration
Similar persons → same treatment
Different persons → different treatment allowed
This is equal protection.
________________________________________
Difference Between Two Concepts
Equality before law:
•	Negative concept 
•	No special privileges 
•	Derived from UK 
Equal protection of laws:
•	Positive concept 
•	Reasonable classification allowed 
•	Derived from USA 
Both together ensure fairness.
________________________________________
Scope of Article 14
Article 14 prohibits arbitrariness. State action must be reasonable. Any arbitrary law violates Article 14. The Article applies to legislative and executive actions.
Article 14 also ensures fairness in administration. Government decisions must be rational. Discrimination without reason is invalid.
Thus, Article 14 promotes equality and fairness.
________________________________________
Conceptual Importance
Article 14:
•	Equality before law 
•	Equal protection of laws 
•	Applies to all persons 
•	Prevents arbitrariness 
•	Allows reasonable classification 
This Article forms foundation of Right to Equality.

Article 14 of the Constitution of India guarantees equality before law and equal protection of laws. However, equality does not mean that all persons must be treated identically. The State can classify persons into different groups. This is called reasonable classification. Such classification must be valid and not arbitrary.
The courts developed the doctrine of reasonable classification to determine whether a law violates Article 14. If classification is reasonable, the law is valid. If classification is arbitrary, it violates Article 14.
________________________________________
Reasonable Classification
Reasonable classification means grouping persons based on intelligible differences. The State may treat different groups differently if there is a valid basis. However, classification must not be arbitrary.
The Supreme Court laid down two conditions for valid classification:
•	There must be intelligible differentia 
•	Differentia must have rational nexus with object of law 
Both conditions must be satisfied.
________________________________________
Intelligible Differentia
Intelligible differentia means classification must be based on clear distinction. The law must identify a group based on reasonable criteria. The distinction must not be vague.
For example, classification based on income level is valid. But classification based on arbitrary criteria is invalid.
________________________________________
Example
Higher tax for rich people is valid classification. Income level is clear distinction.
________________________________________
Rational Nexus
There must be connection between classification and objective of law. Classification should help achieve purpose. If classification has no relation to objective, it is invalid.
For example, if law gives benefits to farmers, classification must relate to agriculture. Otherwise, it violates Article 14.
________________________________________
Illustration
Classification → clear distinction
Distinction → connected to objective
This is reasonable classification.
________________________________________
Doctrine of Arbitrariness
Article 14 also prohibits arbitrariness. State action must be fair and reasonable. Arbitrary laws violate equality. Even without classification, arbitrary action is unconstitutional.
The Supreme Court expanded Article 14 to include fairness. Any irrational or unreasonable law violates Article 14. This widened scope of equality.
Thus, Article 14 not only prohibits discrimination but also arbitrary action.
________________________________________
Example
If government selects persons randomly without criteria, it is arbitrary and violates Article 14.
________________________________________
Case Law – E.P. Royappa Case
Facts:
A government officer challenged his transfer alleging arbitrariness. He argued that transfer violated equality.
Judgment:
The Supreme Court held that arbitrariness is antithetical to equality. Arbitrary state action violates Article 14. Equality and arbitrariness cannot coexist.
Significance:
This case expanded scope of Article 14. It introduced doctrine of arbitrariness.
________________________________________
Case Law – Maneka Gandhi Case
Facts:
Passport of Maneka Gandhi was impounded without reasons. She challenged the action.
Judgment:
The Supreme Court held that arbitrary procedure violates Article 14. Law must be fair and reasonable.
Significance:
Article 14 includes fairness and reasonableness.
________________________________________
Conceptual Importance
Reasonable classification:
•	Intelligible differentia 
•	Rational nexus 
Doctrine of arbitrariness:
•	No arbitrary state action 
•	Fairness required 
These doctrines define scope of Article 14.

Article 15 of the Constitution of India prohibits discrimination by the State. It ensures equality and prevents unfair treatment. While Article 14 provides general equality, Article 15 specifically prohibits discrimination on certain grounds. It strengthens equality by identifying prohibited grounds.
Article 15(1) states that the State shall not discriminate against any citizen on grounds only of:
•	Religion 
•	Race 
•	Caste 
•	Sex 
•	Place of birth 
Thus, discrimination based solely on these grounds is prohibited. Article 15 applies only to citizens, unlike Article 14 which applies to all persons.
________________________________________
Scope of Article 15(1)
Article 15(1) prohibits discrimination by the State. This means government cannot deny benefits or privileges based on the listed grounds. It ensures equal access to opportunities.
However, the word “only” is important. If classification is based on additional factors, it may be valid. For example, residence plus income may be valid classification.
Thus, Article 15 prevents discrimination but allows reasonable classification.
________________________________________
Example
Government cannot deny admission in school only because of caste. This violates Article 15.
________________________________________
Article 15(2) – Access to Public Places
Article 15(2) extends protection against discrimination in public places. It prohibits discrimination by both State and private individuals. It ensures access to:
•	Shops 
•	Restaurants 
•	Hotels 
•	Public wells 
•	Roads 
•	Public places 
This provision prevents social discrimination. It promotes equality in public life.
________________________________________
Example
A restaurant cannot refuse service based on religion. This violates Article 15(2).
________________________________________
Exceptions under Article 15
The Constitution allows certain exceptions. These are not considered discrimination. They aim to promote equality.
Article 15(3) – Special Provisions for Women and Children
The State may make special provisions for women and children. This allows protective laws. Such provisions do not violate equality.
________________________________________
Example
Reservation for women in local bodies is valid.
________________________________________
Article 15(4) – Backward Classes
The State may make special provisions for socially and educationally backward classes. This includes Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes. Reservation in education is allowed.
This provision promotes substantive equality.
________________________________________
Article 15(5)
The State may provide reservation in educational institutions, including private institutions (except minority institutions). This strengthens access to education.
________________________________________
Article 15(6)
This clause allows reservation for economically weaker sections. It provides benefits based on economic criteria.

Articles 17 and 18 of the Constitution of India form part of the Right to Equality. These provisions aim to remove social inequality and establish equal status. Article 17 abolishes untouchability, while Article 18 abolishes titles. Both provisions promote social justice and equality.
________________________________________
Article 17 – Abolition of Untouchability
Article 17 abolishes untouchability and forbids its practice in any form. It declares that enforcement of any disability arising out of untouchability shall be an offence punishable by law. This provision aims to eliminate caste-based discrimination.
Untouchability refers to social practice where certain groups were treated as inferior. They were denied access to public places, temples, and facilities. Article 17 abolishes such practices. It ensures equality and dignity.
Article 17 is absolute. No exception is allowed. Untouchability in any form is prohibited. Parliament enacted laws to enforce Article 17.
________________________________________
Protection of Civil Rights Act, 1955
To implement Article 17, Parliament enacted Protection of Civil Rights Act. This law punishes denial of access to public places. It criminalizes untouchability practices.
Later, the Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (Prevention of Atrocities) Act was also enacted. This provides stronger protection.
________________________________________
Example
Denying entry to temple based on caste is punishable offence.
________________________________________
Illustration
Untouchability → prohibited
Violation → punishable
This is Article 17.
________________________________________
Article 18 – Abolition of Titles
Article 18 abolishes titles. Titles create artificial distinctions and inequality. The Constitution prohibits granting of titles by the State. This promotes equality.
Article 18(1) prohibits the State from conferring titles except military and academic distinctions. Titles like “Raja”, “Nawab”, or “Sir” cannot be granted.
Article 18(2) prohibits citizens from accepting titles from foreign states. This prevents loyalty conflicts.
Article 18(3) prohibits foreign nationals holding office under State from accepting foreign titles without President’s consent.
Article 18(4) prohibits acceptance of foreign gifts without consent.
________________________________________
Example
Government cannot confer hereditary titles. But degrees like “Doctor” are allowed.
________________________________________
National Awards
National awards like Bharat Ratna and Padma awards are allowed. These are not considered titles. They do not create privileges. They are only recognition of merit.
The Supreme Court clarified that these awards do not violate Article 18.
________________________________________
Illustration
Titles → prohibited
Awards → permitted
________________________________________

•	Abolishes untouchability 
•	Punishable offence 
•	Promotes equality 
Article 18:
•	Abolishes titles 
•	Allows academic and military distinctions 
•	National awards permitted 
These provisions promote social equality.

Article 20 of the Constitution of India provides protection to persons accused of crimes. It safeguards individuals against arbitrary punishment. Article 20 applies to both citizens and non-citizens. It protects personal liberty in criminal law.
Article 20 provides three protections:
•	Protection against ex post facto law 
•	Protection against double jeopardy 
•	Protection against self-incrimination 
These protections ensure fairness in criminal justice.
________________________________________
Article 20(1) – Ex Post Facto Law
Article 20(1) prohibits conviction under ex post facto law. Ex post facto law means a law that punishes an act which was not an offence at the time it was committed. A person cannot be punished for an act that was not illegal when done.
This clause also prohibits enhanced punishment retrospectively. If punishment is increased later, it cannot apply to past acts.
This provision ensures fairness. People must know consequences of actions beforehand.
________________________________________
Example
If an act was legal in 2020 but made illegal in 2022, a person cannot be punished for doing it in 2020.
________________________________________
Article 20(2) – Double Jeopardy
Article 20(2) provides that no person shall be prosecuted and punished for same offence more than once. This is called double jeopardy. Once a person is convicted or acquitted, he cannot be tried again for same offence.
This protects individuals from repeated prosecution. It ensures finality of judgment.
However, this protection applies only when:
•	Same offence 
•	Same person 
•	Prosecution and punishment 
________________________________________
Example
If a person is acquitted in theft case, he cannot be tried again for same theft.
________________________________________
Article 20(3) – Self-Incrimination
Article 20(3) provides that no person accused of offence shall be compelled to be witness against himself. This protects against self-incrimination. Accused cannot be forced to confess.
This ensures fair trial. Confession must be voluntary. Police cannot compel accused to give evidence against himself.
However, this protection applies only to accused persons.
________________________________________
Example
Police cannot force accused to confess crime.
________________________________________
Case Law – Nandini Satpathy Case
Facts:
A former Chief Minister was asked to answer questions during investigation. She refused claiming protection against self-incrimination.
Judgment:
The Supreme Court held that accused has right to remain silent. Compulsion violates Article 20(3).
Significance:
Right against self-incrimination includes right to silence.
________________________________________
Conceptual Importance
Article 20:
•	No retrospective punishment 
•	No double punishment 
•	No self-incrimination 
These protections ensure fairness in criminal law.
UNIT 4 – FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS
Article 21 of the Constitution of India is one of the most important Fundamental Rights. It provides that no person shall be deprived of his life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law. This Article protects life and liberty of individuals. It applies to both citizens and non-citizens.
Article 21 originally had narrow interpretation. It meant that life and liberty could be taken away if there was a valid law. Later, the Supreme Court expanded its scope. Now Article 21 includes various rights necessary for dignified life.
________________________________________
Meaning of “Life”
The word “life” does not mean mere animal existence. It means right to live with dignity. Life includes basic necessities such as food, shelter, and health. Courts interpreted life broadly.
Right to life includes:
•	Right to livelihood 
•	Right to health 
•	Right to clean environment 
•	Right to shelter 
•	Right to privacy 
•	Right to dignity 
Thus, Article 21 covers many aspects of life.
________________________________________
Example
Right to clean air is part of right to life.
________________________________________
Personal Liberty
Personal liberty means freedom of person. It includes freedom from unlawful detention. It protects physical liberty. No person can be arrested arbitrarily.
Personal liberty includes:
•	Freedom of movement 
•	Freedom from illegal detention 
•	Right to travel abroad 
•	Right to privacy 
This ensures protection from arbitrary state action.
________________________________________
Example
Illegal detention violates personal liberty.
________________________________________
Procedure Established by Law
Article 21 states that deprivation must follow procedure established by law. Initially, courts interpreted this narrowly. Any law passed by legislature was sufficient.
Later, courts held that procedure must be fair, just, and reasonable. Arbitrary law violates Article 21. This expanded protection.
________________________________________
Case Law – Maneka Gandhi Case
Facts:
Passport of Maneka Gandhi was impounded without giving reasons. She challenged action.
Judgment:
The Supreme Court held that procedure must be fair, just, and reasonable. Article 21 must be read with Articles 14 and 19.
Significance:
Article 21 expanded. Arbitrary procedure invalid.
________________________________________
Expanded Rights under Article 21
Courts recognized several rights under Article 21:
•	Right to privacy 
•	Right to legal aid 
•	Right to speedy trial 
•	Right to clean environment 
•	Right to education (later Article 21A) 
•	Right to livelihood 
This expansion made Article 21 very broad.
________________________________________
Illustration
Life = dignified life
Liberty = personal freedom
Procedure = fair and reasonable
This is Article 21.
________________________________________
Conceptual Importance
Article 21:
•	Protects life 
•	Protects liberty 
•	Fair procedure required 
•	Applies to all persons 
This Article is backbone of Fundamental Rights.
PART 14
UNIT 4 – FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS
Article 21A of the Constitution of India guarantees the Right to Education. It provides that the State shall provide free and compulsory education to all children of the age of six to fourteen years. This right was inserted by the 86th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2002. It made education a fundamental right.
Before this amendment, right to education was part of Directive Principles. Courts later recognized education as part of right to life under Article 21. To give constitutional status, Article 21A was inserted. This ensures that education becomes enforceable right.
Article 21A imposes duty on the State. Government must provide free education. Children between 6 and 14 years must be educated. This promotes literacy and development.
________________________________________
Meaning of Free and Compulsory Education
Free education means that no child shall pay fees. The State bears cost of education. Children from poor families can access education.
Compulsory education means it is obligation of the State to ensure children attend school. Government must create schools and facilities. Parents and guardians must send children to school.
Thus, Article 21A ensures universal elementary education.
________________________________________
Example
Government schools providing free education to children reflect Article 21A.
________________________________________
Scope of Article 21A
Article 21A applies only to children aged 6–14 years. It covers elementary education. The State must provide infrastructure, teachers, and facilities.
This right applies to all children. No discrimination is allowed. Education must be accessible.
Article 21A also led to enactment of Right to Education Act 2009. This law implements the right. It provides norms for schools.
________________________________________
Relationship with Article 21
Right to education was earlier derived from Article 21. Courts held education essential for dignified life. Article 21A made it explicit. Thus, Article 21A is extension of Article 21.
________________________________________
Illustration
Right to life → includes education
Amendment → Article 21A inserted
________________________________________
Importance of Right to Education
Article 21A:
•	Promotes literacy 
•	Ensures equality 
•	Helps social development 
•	Empowers children 
•	Reduces child labour 
Education helps individuals develop personality. It strengthens democracy.
________________________________________
Conceptual Importance
Article 21A:
•	Free education 
•	Compulsory education 
•	Age 6–14 years 
•	Duty of State 
This Article ensures educational rights for children.
PART 15
UNIT 4 – FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS
Article 22 of the Constitution of India provides protection against arbitrary arrest and detention. This Article safeguards personal liberty. It ensures that a person arrested is treated fairly. Article 22 applies to both citizens and non-citizens.
Article 22 deals with two types of detention:
•	Arrest under ordinary law 
•	Preventive detention 
The Constitution provides safeguards in both situations.
________________________________________
Protection in Case of Ordinary Arrest
Article 22(1) provides that a person arrested must be informed of grounds of arrest. This ensures that the person knows why he is arrested. Without information, arrest is illegal.
Article 22(1) also provides right to consult and be defended by legal practitioner. This ensures fair defence. The arrested person can contact lawyer.
Article 22(2) provides that arrested person must be produced before magistrate within 24 hours. Detention beyond 24 hours requires judicial approval. This prevents illegal detention.
________________________________________
Example
Police arrest a person → must inform reasons → produce before magistrate within 24 hours.
________________________________________
Illustration
Arrest → inform grounds → lawyer allowed → produce before magistrate
This is protection.
________________________________________
Exceptions to Article 22(1) and (2)
These protections do not apply to:
•	Enemy aliens 
•	Preventive detention cases 
Thus, preventive detention has separate rules.
________________________________________
Preventive Detention
Preventive detention means detention without trial to prevent future offences. A person may be detained to prevent threat to security. This is precautionary detention.
Though preventive detention restricts liberty, Constitution permits it. However, safeguards exist.
Article 22(4) provides that detention beyond three months requires advisory board approval. Advisory board examines detention. It ensures fairness.
Article 22(5) provides that detained person must be informed grounds. He must be given opportunity to make representation.
However, authorities may withhold facts in public interest.
________________________________________
Example
Person suspected of threatening security may be detained preventively.
________________________________________
Importance of Article 22
Article 22:
•	Protects against arbitrary arrest 
•	Ensures legal representation 
•	Requires production before magistrate 
•	Regulates preventive detention 
This Article balances liberty and security.
________________________________________
Conceptual Understanding
Ordinary arrest:
•	Inform grounds 
•	Lawyer allowed 
•	Magistrate within 24 hours 
Preventive detention:
•	Advisory board 
•	Representation allowed 
•	Limited safeguards 
Thus, Article 22 protects personal liberty.
PART 16
UNIT 4 – FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS
Articles 23 and 24 of the Constitution of India provide the Right Against Exploitation. These provisions protect individuals from forced labour and child labour. They aim to eliminate exploitation and ensure human dignity. These rights apply to both citizens and non-citizens.
________________________________________
Article 23 – Prohibition of Traffic in Human Beings and Forced Labour
Article 23 prohibits:
•	Traffic in human beings 
•	Begar 
•	Forced labour 
Traffic in human beings refers to selling and buying of persons. It includes human trafficking for labour or prostitution. This practice is prohibited.
Begar means forced labour without payment. In the past, people were forced to work without wages. Article 23 abolishes such practices.
Forced labour means compelling a person to work against his will. Even if payment is given, forcing someone to work is prohibited.
Article 23 is enforceable against both State and private individuals. This is important. Private persons cannot exploit others.
________________________________________
Example
Forcing labourers to work without wages violates Article 23.
________________________________________
Illustration
Forced work → prohibited
Human trafficking → prohibited
This is Article 23.
________________________________________
Exception under Article 23
Article 23 allows compulsory service for public purposes. The State may require citizens to perform public service. However, no discrimination is allowed.
________________________________________
Example
Compulsory military service is valid.
________________________________________
Article 24 – Prohibition of Child Labour
Article 24 prohibits employment of children below 14 years in hazardous industries. This protects children from exploitation. Children should focus on education and development.
Hazardous industries include factories, mines, and dangerous occupations. Employment in such sectors is prohibited.
Article 24 promotes welfare of children. It complements right to education.
________________________________________
Example
Child working in factory violates Article 24.
________________________________________
Importance of Right Against Exploitation
Articles 23 and 24:
•	Protect human dignity 
•	Prevent forced labour 
•	Prohibit child labour 
•	Eliminate exploitation 
These provisions promote social justice.
________________________________________
Conceptual Understanding
Article 23:
•	No human trafficking 
•	No forced labour 
•	Applies to private persons 
Article 24:
•	No child labour below 14 
•	Hazardous industries prohibited 
These Articles protect individuals from exploitation.
PART 17
UNIT 4 – FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS
Articles 25 to 28 of the Constitution of India guarantee freedom of religion. India is a secular state. These provisions ensure that every person can follow religion freely. The State does not favour any religion. These rights promote religious harmony.
The right to freedom of religion includes:
•	Freedom of conscience 
•	Freedom to profess religion 
•	Freedom to practice religion 
•	Freedom to propagate religion 
These rights are subject to public order, morality, and health.
________________________________________
Article 25 – Freedom of Conscience
Article 25 guarantees freedom of conscience. Every person has right to believe in any religion. It also allows profess, practice, and propagate religion.
Profess means declaring faith.
Practice means performing rituals.
Propagate means spreading religious beliefs.
This right applies to all persons, not only citizens. However, State may regulate secular activities.
________________________________________
Example
A person following any religion freely is protected under Article 25.
________________________________________
Article 26 – Freedom to Manage Religious Affairs
Article 26 gives rights to religious denominations. They can:
•	Establish religious institutions 
•	Manage religious affairs 
•	Own property 
•	Administer property 
This ensures autonomy of religious groups. However, this right is subject to public order and morality.
________________________________________
Example
Religious institutions managing temples or mosques is protected.
________________________________________
Article 27 – Freedom from Religious Taxes
Article 27 provides that no person shall be compelled to pay taxes for promotion of any religion. The State cannot use tax money to promote religion. This ensures secularism.
However, taxes for general purposes are allowed.
________________________________________
Example
Government cannot impose tax for promoting particular religion.
________________________________________
Article 28 – Religious Instruction in Educational Institutions
Article 28 deals with religious instruction in educational institutions.
It provides:
•	No religious instruction in institutions wholly funded by State 
•	Religious instruction allowed in institutions administered by trusts 
•	Students cannot be compelled to attend religious instruction 
This protects freedom of belief.
________________________________________
Example
Government school cannot compel students to attend religious prayer.
________________________________________
Conceptual Importance
Article 25:
•	Freedom of conscience 
•	Practice religion 
Article 26:
•	Manage religious affairs 
Article 27:
•	No religious taxes 
Article 28:
•	No compulsory religious instruction 
These Articles ensure religious freedom.
PART 18
UNIT 4 – FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS
Articles 29 and 30 of the Constitution of India provide Cultural and Educational Rights. These rights protect interests of minorities. India is a diverse country with different languages, religions, and cultures. These provisions ensure that minorities can preserve their identity.
These Articles aim to protect cultural diversity. They also prevent discrimination in educational institutions.
________________________________________
Article 29 – Protection of Interests of Minorities
Article 29(1) provides that any section of citizens having distinct language, script, or culture has right to conserve it. This right is available to both majority and minority groups. Any group with distinct identity can protect culture.
This provision ensures preservation of cultural heritage. It allows communities to maintain language and traditions.
________________________________________
Example
A linguistic group preserving its language is protected under Article 29.
________________________________________
Article 29(2) – Admission in Educational Institutions
Article 29(2) provides that no citizen shall be denied admission in educational institution maintained by State on grounds of:
•	Religion 
•	Race 
•	Caste 
•	Language 
This prevents discrimination in education. It ensures equal access.
________________________________________
Example
A college cannot deny admission based on religion. This violates Article 29(2).
________________________________________
Article 30 – Right of Minorities to Establish Educational Institutions
Article 30 provides that minorities have right to establish and administer educational institutions. Minorities may be based on religion or language. This right ensures educational autonomy.
Minorities can establish schools and colleges. They can manage administration. Government cannot interfere unnecessarily.
Article 30 also provides that State shall not discriminate in granting aid to minority institutions.
________________________________________
Example
Religious minority establishing school is protected under Article 30.
________________________________________
Difference Between Article 29 and 30
Article 29:
•	Protects culture 
•	Applies to any section 
Article 30:
•	Protects minorities 
•	Right to establish institutions 
Both protect diversity.
________________________________________
Conceptual Importance
Article 29:
•	Preserve language 
•	No discrimination in admission 
Article 30:
•	Minority educational institutions 
•	Administrative autonomy 
These Articles protect cultural diversity.
PART 19
UNIT 4 – FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS
Article 32 of the Constitution of India provides the Right to Constitutional Remedies. It is considered the heart and soul of Fundamental Rights. This Article allows individuals to approach the Supreme Court directly for enforcement of Fundamental Rights. Without remedies, rights become meaningless. Therefore, Article 32 ensures protection.
Article 32 guarantees that if Fundamental Rights are violated, a person can move the Supreme Court. The Court may issue directions, orders, or writs. This makes Fundamental Rights enforceable.
Dr. B.R. Ambedkar described Article 32 as the most important Article. It protects all other rights.
________________________________________
Power of Supreme Court
Under Article 32, Supreme Court has power to:
•	Issue writs 
•	Enforce Fundamental Rights 
•	Provide remedies 
This power is guaranteed right. Citizens can directly approach Supreme Court. The Court cannot refuse.
Article 32 itself is Fundamental Right. Therefore, denial of remedy violates Constitution.
________________________________________
Example
If freedom of speech is violated, person can directly approach Supreme Court.
________________________________________
Types of Writs
The Supreme Court may issue five types of writs:
•	Habeas Corpus 
•	Mandamus 
•	Prohibition 
•	Certiorari 
•	Quo Warranto 
These writs protect Fundamental Rights.
________________________________________
Habeas Corpus
Habeas Corpus means “produce the body”. It is issued when a person is illegally detained. Court orders authority to produce detained person. If detention is illegal, person is released.
________________________________________
Example
Illegal arrest → Court issues Habeas Corpus → person released.
________________________________________
Mandamus
Mandamus means “we command”. Court directs public authority to perform duty. If authority fails to perform legal duty, writ is issued.
________________________________________
Example
Authority refusing to grant license illegally → Mandamus issued.
________________________________________
Prohibition
Prohibition is issued to prevent lower court from exceeding jurisdiction. Higher court orders lower court to stop proceedings.
________________________________________
Certiorari
Certiorari is issued to quash order of lower court. If lower court acts without jurisdiction, order is cancelled.
________________________________________
Quo Warranto
Quo Warranto means “by what authority”. Court questions authority of person holding public office. If appointment invalid, person removed.
________________________________________
Example
Person holding office illegally → Quo Warranto issued.
________________________________________
Importance of Article 32
Article 32:
•	Enforces Fundamental Rights 
•	Allows direct access to Supreme Court 
•	Provides writ remedies 
•	Protects liberty 
Thus, Article 32 is cornerstone of Fundamental Rights.
PART 20
UNIT 4 – FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS
Articles 33 to 35 of the Constitution of India deal with miscellaneous provisions relating to Fundamental Rights. These provisions explain restrictions, modification, and implementation of Fundamental Rights. They complete Part III of the Constitution.
________________________________________
Article 33 – Power of Parliament to Modify Fundamental Rights
Article 33 empowers Parliament to restrict or modify Fundamental Rights for certain forces. These include:
•	Armed forces 
•	Police forces 
•	Intelligence agencies 
•	Forces maintaining public order 
The purpose is to ensure discipline. Members of these forces cannot exercise all freedoms freely. Restrictions ensure effective functioning.
________________________________________
Example
Military personnel cannot freely protest. This restriction is valid.
________________________________________
Article 34 – Restriction during Martial Law
Article 34 provides that Fundamental Rights may be restricted in areas where martial law is in force. Martial law refers to military control in disturbed areas. During such situations, certain rights may be restricted.
This provision protects actions taken during emergency situations. It allows Parliament to indemnify officials.
________________________________________
Example
During martial law, movement may be restricted.
________________________________________
Article 35 – Legislation to Give Effect to Fundamental Rights
Article 35 provides that Parliament has exclusive power to make laws relating to certain Fundamental Rights. These include:
•	Article 16 (residence requirement) 
•	Article 32 (writ jurisdiction) 
•	Article 33 (modification of rights) 
•	Article 34 (martial law) 
Only Parliament can legislate on these matters. States cannot pass such laws.
This ensures uniformity.
________________________________________
Importance of Articles 33–35
These provisions:
•	Allow modification of rights 
•	Maintain discipline in forces 
•	Regulate emergency situations 
•	Empower Parliament 
They complete Fundamental Rights framework.
________________________________________
Overall Conclusion of Unit 4
Fundamental Rights form core of the Constitution. They protect liberty, equality, and dignity. These rights limit state power and ensure democratic governance. Courts enforce these rights through judicial review.
Fundamental Rights include:
•	Right to Equality 
•	Right to Freedom 
•	Right against Exploitation 
•	Freedom of Religion 
•	Cultural and Educational Rights 
•	Constitutional Remedies 
These rights ensure protection of individuals and promote rule of law.
