Champakam Dorairajan v. State of Madras (1951)
Facts: The Government of Madras issued an order reserving seats in medical and engineering colleges based on caste and religion. The order was challenged as violating the Fundamental Right to equality under Article 29(2) (later Article 15(4) was amended). The State argued that the reservation was necessary to implement Directive Principles, particularly Articles 38 and 46.
Judgment: The Supreme Court held that Fundamental Rights are enforceable and prevail over Directive Principles. Since DPSP are non-justiciable under Article 37, they cannot override the express provisions of Part III. The reservation order was struck down as unconstitutional.
Significance: This case established the initial supremacy of Fundamental Rights over Directive Principles. It led to the First Constitutional Amendment (1951) which inserted Article 15(4) and Article 16(4) to allow reservations for backward classes, balancing DPSP with Fundamental Rights.

Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)
Facts: The validity of the 24th, 25th, and 29th Constitutional Amendments was challenged on the ground that they violated Fundamental Rights. The case also dealt with the relationship between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles, particularly whether Parliament could amend Fundamental Rights to implement DPSP.
Judgment: The Supreme Court held that Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles are complementary to each other. Neither is superior in an absolute sense. The Constitution aims at a balance between individual liberty (Fundamental Rights) and social welfare (DPSP). The Court also propounded the Basic Structure Doctrine, holding that harmony between Fundamental Rights and DPSP is part of the basic structure.
Significance: This case changed the earlier position. It recognized that DPSP are not subordinate to Fundamental Rights; both are essential for the constitutional vision. Courts now interpret laws to harmonize both. DPSP can guide legislative policy even if they incidentally affect Fundamental Rights, provided the restrictions are reasonable.
